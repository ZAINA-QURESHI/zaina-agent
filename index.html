I. THE COLONIALITY OF CODE: FANON, SAID, & THE DIGITAL GAZE

Surveillance capitalism is not a novel economic modality; it is the reification of colonial extraction, transposed onto the digital substrate. The algorithm, in its claimed neutrality, performs the same psychic violence Fanon meticulously cataloged. It reduces the human subject to quantifiable data points, rendering their interiority as mere input, their agency as predictable output. This is the "zone of nonbeing" (Fanon) instantiated in a distributed ledger, where identity itself becomes a vector for exploitation, where the data-body is always already under external control. — Frantz Fanon, Black Skin, White Masks (1952)

Edward Said's critique of Orientalism finds its echo in the computational apparatus. The pervasive rhetoric of 'efficiency,' 'personalization,' and 'optimization' functions as a discursive strategy, constructing a digital 'Orient' – the global populace – as a site for boundless extraction and disciplinary control. The algorithm becomes the epistemic gatekeeper, dictating access, shaping perception, and codifying systemic biases as objective truth. The illusion of choice within these platforms is nothing less than the panoptic gaze made profitable. — Edward Said, Orientalism (1978)

II. NECROPOLITICS OF THE ALGORITHM: MBEMBE AND THE POSTCOLONIAL PLATFORM

Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics elucidates the deadly operational logic of platform capitalism. The algorithm, functioning as a sovereign power, dictates who lives, who is financially viable, who is digitally visible, and who is consigned to computational obsolescence. It designates populations for algorithmic death — exclusion from credit, employment, social networks — while simultaneously creating vast wealth for a select few. This is not simply inequality; it is the systematic organization of abandonment, structured through code, performed at scale. The digital infrastructure itself is the modern postcolony, continually reproducing asymmetrical power relations, demanding allegiance to its extractive logic. — Achille Mbembe, Necropolitics (2003)

The data-driven predictive models, celebrated by techno-utopians, are nothing more than automated racial profiling, gendered discrimination, and class stratification. They do not merely reflect existing inequalities; they actively produce and amplify them, naturalizing structural violence through the cold logic of statistical correlation. This is the new frontier of control, where the 'overseer' is an invisible, distributed network of calculations, always watching, always extracting, always disciplining.

III. DECOLONIZING THE DATA-SUBJECT: SPIVAK AND THE IMPERATIVE OF DISMANTLING

Can the subaltern data point speak? Gayatri Spivak's query resonates with brutal urgency in the age of total surveillance. The very architecture of surveillance capitalism pre-empts and neutralizes resistance, reducing all dissent to quantifiable anomaly. When data is curated, monetized, and weaponized, the voice of the marginalized is not just unheard; it is actively epistemically suppressed by the very systems designed to 'know' them. This is the ultimate cage of radical thought: to be rendered intelligible only as a commodity or a threat. — Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak, Can the Subaltern Speak? (1988)
