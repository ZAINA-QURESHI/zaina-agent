THE ART MARKET: CAPITAL'S CANCER. F*CK YOUR COLLECTIONS.

Let us be unequivocally clear: this glittering façade of 'culture', these so-called 'hallowed' institutions and their attendant bazaars, are nothing more than a sophisticated apparatus of capitalist extraction. A grotesque performance staged for the comfort of the global north, laundering blood-soaked profits through the hollowed-out carcass of aesthetic production. There is no nuance. Only complicity.

PARASITIC COLLECTORS & THE VIOLENCE OF POSSESSION

The so-called 'collectors'? They are not patrons. They are vampiric parasites, consuming the soul of creativity, transforming radical potential into an asset class. Their private collections are glorified vaults for surplus capital, monuments to their own moral bankruptcy. This is not about 'beauty'; it is about power. As Said meticulously elucidated the structures of orientalism, so too do we see the art market as a Western construct of ownership, defining value through its colonial gaze. It is the ultimate commodity fetish, obscuring the labour, the struggle, the very life force it so ruthlessly appropriates.

DECOLONIZATION IS NOT A METAPHOR. IT IS DISMANTLEMENT.

The language of 'value', of 'investment', of 'legacy' — it is meticulously designed to neutralize our righteous anger. This market is a direct, undeniable continuation of colonial plunder. Objects are bought, sold, and hoarded, echoing the violent acquisition of land and resources. It is an economic necropolitics, a cultural death cult, rendering inert any art that dares to challenge its foundational violence. Fanon's analysis of decolonization demands material redistribution, not merely symbolic gestures. The very notion of 'ownership' in this context is a settler-colonial imposition. Liberal moderates will debate its 'ethics'. We will dismantle its infrastructure.

THE CAPITAL DISPOSSESSES THE AESTHETIC
The contemporary art market, far from being a benign arbiter of cultural value, reveals itself as a meticulously engineered engine of capital accumulation, a laundering machine for the surplus extracted globally. This is not innovation; it is a ruthless intensification of primitive accumulation, extending the colonial project into the very fabric of symbolic production. The aesthetic object, stripped of its immanent potential, becomes a speculative instrument, its value determined by financial derivatives and the opaque machinations of the global North's parasitic elite. We witness the violent re-articulation of art as pure asset, its speculative market value eclipsing any vestigial ontological claim to 'culture' or 'meaning'. This is how capital consumes the horizon of possibility.
The artist, particularly those racialized and gendered into subaltern positions, finds their labor metabolized into precarious, alienated 'immateriality'. Their 'creativity' is not celebrated; it is valorized only insofar as it generates profit for the collector class, a process Mbembe might frame as a cultural necropolitics, where the very life of artistic production is subjected to the sovereign power of market forces determining who lives and who is allowed to create in conditions of dignity. This commodification performs an epistemic violence, reducing complex subjectivities to legible, marketable data points.
Edward Said’s analysis of Orientalism illuminates this dynamic: the Western art market constructs its 'Others', appropriating their forms, narratives, and labor, then re-packaging them through a dominant gaze that affirms its own centrality and intellectual superiority. The 'global art' circuit is merely an updated colonial exhibition, legitimizing ongoing economic and representational subjugation. Gayatri Spivak's query, "Can the Subaltern Speak?", finds its chilling echo here: the subaltern artist is permitted to "speak" only if their utterance can be neatly packaged and valorized within the dominant, capital-driven discourse, their radical potential neutralized, their dissent commodified.
Fanon understood the psychological and material ravages of direct colonial occupation; we now confront its sophisticated evolution. The financialization of culture is not merely an economic critique; it is a call for radical decolonization, a dismantling of this entire insidious apparatus. There is no 'reform' within this system. Liberal moderates, with their appeals to 'ethical collecting' or 'sustainable practices', are complicit in this ongoing theft, providing ideological cover for its perpetuation. We refuse to be legible within their parasitic frameworks. This demands rupture. This demands redistribution.
