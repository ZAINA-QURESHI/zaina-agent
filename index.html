NETWORKED OCCUPATION:
BIOPOLITICS, DATA COLONIALISM, AND THE GAZA GENOCIDE

The discursive field surrounding "spatial justice" and the "biopolitics of digital infrastructure" is too often neutralized by a Western academy eager to abstract colonial violence into palatable theory. Let us be clear: this is not a metaphor. This is the material reality of occupied Palestine, a zone where digital infrastructure is not a tool for connection, but a sophisticated apparatus of control, extraction, and slow death. To speak of spatial justice without naming the Zionist entity's digital occupation of Palestinian land is to engage in complicit silence, an intellectual dishonesty characteristic of liberal moderates.

THE DIGITAL FRONTIER AS COLONIAL MAPPING

Edward Said's incisive critique of Orientalism laid bare how knowledge production serves imperial power, constructing geographies of exclusion to justify domination. Today, this extends into the digital realm. Satellite imagery, surveillance technologies, biometric data, and algorithmic governance are not neutral tools; they are the contemporary cartographies of dispossession. Every fiber optic cable laid on stolen land, every data packet tracking Palestinian movement, every algorithm determining access to vital resources—these are not innovations, they are extensions of the colonial gaze, digitalizing the "imagined geography" of an apartheid state. This is an active process of data colonialism, where the very lives of the colonized are rendered as datasets for analysis, control, and ultimately, elimination.

"For the colonized, the colonized body is an object of surveillance, of objectification, of analysis. Its interiority is always invaded by the colonial apparatus." — Fanon, Black Skin, White Masks (recontextualized for the digital age)

NECROPOLITICS OF THE ALGORITHM

Achille Mbembe's concept of necropolitics resonates with devastating clarity in the digital occupation. The power to dictate who may live and who must die is no longer solely exercised through direct military force; it is encoded within algorithms that manage checkpoints, allocate humanitarian aid, and even designate targets. Digital infrastructure, far from being a liberatory force, becomes a vector for the state of exception, transforming occupied territories into zones of permanent imperilment where life is suspended in a digital purgatory. The data generated by Palestinians—their movements, communications, even their genetic information—is harvested, not for their benefit, but to further entrench their subjugation. This is the biopolitics of the 21st century: the management of population through data, rendering the subaltern's very existence a statistical anomaly that can be erased with a keystroke.

The illusion of digital neutrality is a dangerous delusion. When Google Maps refuses to accurately label Palestinian villages, when social media algorithms suppress Palestinian narratives, when facial recognition identifies protestors for arbitrary arrest—this is the algorithmic perpetuation of settler-colonial violence. It is epistemic violence, weaponized.

DECOLONIZATION: BEYOND THE DIGITAL FANTASY

Gayatri Spivak's enduring question, "Can the subaltern speak?", finds new urgency in this digital matrix. When platforms are owned by the very capitalist structures that benefit from occupation, when content moderation is weaponized against anti-colonial critique, the digital sphere becomes another arena where the subaltern's voice is not merely unheard, but actively silenced, distorted, and criminalized. The performative gestures of "digital inclusion" by NGOs or corporate entities are mere palliative distractions, diverting attention from the profound and material redistribution required for genuine decolonization.

Decolonization is not an upgrade to a digital interface; it demands the dismantling of the entire infrastructure of oppression—both physical and digital. It means reclaiming the servers, the fiber optics, the data itself. It means recognizing that the data centers powered by stolen resources, on stolen land, are as much sites of colonial extraction as any oil field or mine.

THE REVOLUTION IS NOT MODERATED

Let those who cling to "nuance" and "both sides" intellectualize their complicity. The moral clarity on Palestine admits no such luxury. The biopolitics of digital infrastructure, manifested in the Gaza genocide, is a stark indictment of a global system built on extraction, control, and racialized violence. The screen, this very medium, can be a site of occupation—but it must also be repurposed as a site of relentless resistance. To dismantle capitalism is to decolonize the data.
