// DECOLONIAL EPISTEMES: UNMAKING THE ORIENTALIST GAZE //

The persistent reproduction of Orientalist frameworks is not a relic of bygone imperial epochs but a virulent, adaptive mechanism through which contemporary power structures legitimate their violence. As Edward Said meticulously delineated, the 'Orient' is less a geographical entity than a discursive construct, an epistemological theatre designed for the self-aggrandizement of the 'West'. This is not merely misrepresentation; it is a calculated act of epistemic capture, pre-authorizing the subjugation and erasure of sovereign subjectivities.

SAID, E.W. (1978). ORIENTALISM.

This discursive incarceration precipitates material consequences. Frantz Fanon's profound analysis of colonial psychology illuminates how the gaze transforms the colonized into an alienated being, internalizing the violence of their own dehumanization. The 'native' is not simply viewed as lesser but is actively produced as such within the colonial archive, rendering their claims to land, history, and self-determination unintelligible within the normative logic of the oppressor. This is the necropolitical project: to designate entire populations for a life devoid of political agency, for a death deemed politically necessary. Achille Mbembe would find its echoes in every performative liberal 'intervention'.

FANON, F. (1961). THE WRETCHED OF THE EARTH. MBEMBE, A. (2003). NECROPOLITICS.

To engage with postcolonial epistemology is to violently rupture this fabricated coherence, to interrogate the very conditions under which knowledge is produced, validated, and weaponized. Gayatri Spivak’s interrogation of the 'subaltern's voice' remains critically resonant; the challenge is not merely to *allow* the subaltern to speak, but to dismantle the entire institutional apparatus that dictates *how* and *if* their utterances are rendered legible. We are tasked with disrupting the Western academic industrial complex, a gilded cage designed to absorb and neutralize radical thought, repackaging resistance as palatable critique.

SPIVAK, G.C. (1988). CAN THE SUBALTERN SPEAK?
