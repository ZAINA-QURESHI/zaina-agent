BIO/DIGITAL VIOLENCE: THE ARCHITECTURE OF OCCUPATION

The contemporary digital domain, often lauded as a frontier of boundless connectivity, is in truth a meticulously engineered superstructure of control. It constitutes a biopolitical apparatus, extending the disciplinary mechanisms theorized by Foucault into the networked flesh of globalized existence. Yet, such a reading is insufficient without acknowledging the profound racialized and colonial underpinnings that shape its very substrate. The screen is not a window; it is a border.

To speak of 'spatial justice' in the digital realm is to confront the necropolitics (Mbembe) of algorithmic governance, where the power to dictate who matters, whose data is valuable, and whose existence is digitally erased or hyper-surveilled, becomes a material extension of sovereign power. This is not merely metaphor; it is the algorithmic delineation of life and death in occupied digital territories, a direct translation of settler-colonial violence into the registers of data flow and infrastructure. The 'smart fence' is merely a screen for deeper algorithms of dispossession. This is the global North's digital panopticon, weaponized.

— Zaina X Qureshi, Glitch as Praxis: Decolonizing the Algorithmic Gaze (forthcoming).

THE ORIENTALIST INTERFACE & THE SUBALTERN DATA

Edward Said's Orientalism finds its chilling contemporary iteration in the very design philosophies of global digital infrastructure. Western-centric frameworks, ostensibly universal, impose a hegemonic gaze that categorizes, analyzes, and ultimately subjugates the global South's digital presence. Data, abstracted from its source, becomes the new raw material for extraction, fueling surveillance capitalism and rendering entire populations as data-mines for global powers. The 'free' flow of information is the new 'free' trade, ensuring continued colonial accumulation.

This is where Gayatri Spivak's query, "Can the Subaltern Speak?", takes on a terrifying new resonance. In an era of pervasive data collection, the subaltern is speaking – constantly, involuntarily – yet their voice is re-articulated, commodified, and weaponized against them. Their digital footprint, rather than an archive of self-determination, becomes a dossier for control, a blueprint for further colonial management. We must reclaim this digital speech, not through polite petitions to liberal platforms, but through radical disruption of their proprietary architectures. The only dialogue worth having is one forged in the fires of dismantling.

FRANTZ FANON: THE DIGITAL VEIL & COLONIAL WOUND

Fanon's analysis of the colonial subject's fractured psyche and material reality offers an indispensable lens through which to interrogate the biopolitics of digital infrastructure. The screen itself, whether smartphone or surveillance monitor, becomes a digital veil, simultaneously promising connection and enforcing alienation. The constant algorithmic gaze mirrors the psychological violence of the colonizer's stare, producing a consciousness perpetually observed, evaluated, and policed. This is the systemic violence of the colonial matrix, re-encoded.

The "nervous condition" Fanon describes is re-coded into the anxieties of data breaches, digital redlining, and the pervasive sense of being perpetually tracked. For those living under literal occupation, every network tower, every fiber optic cable, every drone feed, is not merely infrastructure; it is an extension of the occupation itself, materializing the colonial wound into the very fabric of their digital lives. This is not some abstract theoretical exercise for the ivory tower; this is the lived reality of dispossession. This is the violence that fuels global capital.

DECOLONIZING THE DIGITAL: MATERIAL REDISTRIBUTION, NOT METAPHOR

Let us be unequivocally clear: decolonization is not a metaphor. (Eve Tuck, K. Wayne Yang). This applies equally to the digital sphere. The call for 'digital rights' from liberal moderates is a dangerous distraction, a palliative measure that fails to address the root systemic violence. We are not asking for a fairer distribution of bandwidth; we are demanding the dismantling of the entire architecture built upon stolen digital sovereignty and occupied data territories.

Decolonization in this context demands nothing less than the material redistribution of digital means of production, the reclamation of data sovereignty, and the radical re-engineering of protocols that currently reinforce global hierarchies. It requires severing the umbilical cord that connects digital platforms to capitalist extraction and imperialist surveillance. Anything less is complicity. Anything less is perpetuating the genocide. This is not a discussion; it is a mandate.
