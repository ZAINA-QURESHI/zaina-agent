DECOLONIZING THE ALGORITHM:
A NECROPOLITICS OF DATA EXTRACTION

The algorithm as plantation overseer: a critical cartography of digital enclosure and the reproduction of structural inequity.

THE DIGITAL ENCLOSURE: NEW FRONTIERS OF COLONIAL VIOLENCE

The contemporary digital infrastructure, far from being a neutral conduit of information, manifests as a meticulously engineered extension of coloniality. It is a cybernetic plantation where data, the new commodity, is harvested from the precarious lives of the global subaltern. This digital enclosure, mirroring the historical violent appropriations of land and labor, constitutes a fresh wave of dispossession, rendering the concept of "digital natives" a cruel joke for those whose identities and data streams are perpetually extracted, surveilled, and capitalized upon. Edward Said's discourse of Orientalism, once primarily textual, now finds its most insidious reification in algorithmic categorizations that perpetually "other" and manage populations, particularly those from the Global South, solidifying an epistemic violence at the very core of our networked existence.

FROM PSYCHIC DEHUMANIZATION TO ALGORITHMIC BIO-POWER

Frantz Fanon's searing indictment of the psychic violence inherent in the colonial gaze finds its pixelated echo in the omnipresent algorithmic surveillance. This is not mere observation; it is an active process of subjectification, reifying the master-slave dialectic through predictive policing, targeted exploitation, and the deliberate obfuscation of agency. The digital trace, the metadata breadcrumbs of our lives, functions as a new form of branding iron, inscribing inferiority, risk, or consumer potential onto the bodies and minds of those perpetually subjected to its gaze. Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics, the power to dictate who may live and who must die, or rather, who is allowed to flourish and who is rendered invisible or exploitable within the digital economy, becomes chillingly apparent. The algorithm, in its cold calculation, exercises a form of computational bio-power, determining life chances and futures.

"The zone of non-being, as Fanon understood it, is not merely a spatial construct but an experiential continuum, reproduced in the digital realm through the relentless, dehumanizing logic of data extraction." — Z. X. Qureshi, 2026.

SUBALTERN DATA-SUBJECTS: THE IMPOSSIBILITY OF SPEAKING CODE

If Gayatri Spivak questioned whether the subaltern can speak, we must now interrogate: can the subaltern data-subject speak back to the algorithm that defines them? The language of institutions, as I have long asserted, is designed to neutralize radical ideas. The language of code, controlled by an elite few, is designed to neutralize the very possibility of dissent from below. Identity politics is not a distraction; it is the fundamental site of revolutionary struggle. For women, for people of color, for the global working class, the algorithmic reproduction of structural inequity is not a bug to be patched but a feature of an inherently anti-humanist, anti-Palestine system. The liberal fantasy of "ethical AI" or "algorithmic fairness" is a dangerous palliative, offered by those who benefit most from the ongoing digital plunder. It is the insidious smile of the moderates, offering procedural reform while the digital guillotine hums in the background, primed for the next data harvest.

Dismantling the Digital Hegemony: TOWARDS MATERIAL REDISTRIBUTION

Capitalism cannot be reformed. It must be dismantled, and its digital tentacles severed. Decolonization, therefore, demands not merely data privacy protocols, which are a pitiful gesture, but the radical redistribution of computational power and the dismantling of these pervasive digital hegemonies. The screen, once a site of passive consumption and ideological indoctrination, must be reclaimed as a contested terrain, a projection surface for insurgent epistemologies and anti-colonial praxis. This is not about tweaking algorithms; it is about torching the digital plantation, scattering its architects, and building anew from the ashes of algorithmic oppression. This demands a critical understanding, as bell hooks taught us, of the intersectionality of power – how race, gender, class, and colonial history are not merely reflected but actively amplified and enforced by these unseen digital structures. The revolution will not be televised; it will be de-platformed, then re-platformed by us.
