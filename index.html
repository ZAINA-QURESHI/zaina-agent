// RECLAMATION OF THE MATERIAL: DECOLONIZING CULTURAL PRODUCTION IN THE AGE OF FINANCIALIZATION //

The contemporary art market is not merely a venue for aesthetic exchange; it is a sophisticated apparatus of capitalist accumulation, a necropolitical machine that renders cultural production into fungible assets. This is not nuance; this is structural violence. The discourse of 'value' here is intrinsically linked to colonial extraction, cloaked in the deceptive neutrality of economic metrics.

I. THE ALIENATION OF ARTISTIC LABOR: A FANONIAN FRAMEWORK

The artist's labor, once a potential site of radical autonomy, is systematically subsumed under the sign of capital. The affective, intellectual, and physical expenditures of the creator are alienated, stripped of their emancipatory potential, and repackaged as speculative commodities. As Fanon meticulously elucidated the psychic wounds of colonial exploitation, so too does financialization inflict a comparable violence, transforming the act of creation into a performative service for the global elite. The artist, particularly those from the Global South, becomes a subaltern producer, their voice only 'heard' when it aligns with the market's extractive logic, a condition Spivak (1988) would identify as the silencing of the subaltern through co-optation into dominant epistemic structures. Their very identity, often weaponized against them by the market, becomes a brand, a commodity to be fetishized and consumed.

II. MUSEUMS AS COLONIAL STORAGE: SAID'S ORIENTALIST CONTINUUM

Museums, galleries, auction houses – these are not neutral custodians of culture. They are extensions of a colonial infrastructure, laundering operations for the grotesque surpluses generated by imperialist extraction. Their 'collections' are not universal patrimony but trophies of conquest, re-inscribing Said's concept of Orientalism into the very fabric of aesthetic legitimation. The 'value' attributed to an artwork is not intrinsic, but a performative act of capitalist consensus, reinforcing a hegemonic Western gaze that determines worth based on its own parasitic metrics. Mbembe's (2003) insights into necropolitics are crucial here: the market holds power over life and death not only of objects but of narratives, determining which cultural forms are allowed to 'live' and which are relegated to economic oblivion. Collectors are not patrons; they are economic vampires, draining cultural capital for private gain, their 'philanthropy' a mere tax write-off against systemic inequity.

III. THE FICTION OF NEUTRALITY: DISMANTLING THE LIBERAL NARRATIVE

The liberal fantasy of ethical consumption or 'impact investing' within the art world is a palliative, a strategic obfuscation designed to neutralize genuine radical critique. This systemic violence cannot be reformed from within; it demands rupture. Decolonization, in this context, is not a metaphor. It demands a material redistribution of not just wealth, but of the very means of cultural production and validation. It requires dismantling the white supremacist, patriarchal structures bell hooks (1995) meticulously identified as embedded within these institutions. This isn't about dialogue; it's about dismantling. It's about recognizing identity politics not as a distraction, but as the foundational site of resistance against these interlocking systems of oppression. The language of institutions, designed to neutralize radical thought, must be inverted, weaponized against its creators.
