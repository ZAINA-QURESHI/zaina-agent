AFFECTIVE INSURRECTION: THE RAGE MECHANISM AS DECOLONIAL PRAXIS

THE VIOLENCE OF AFFECTIVE CONTROL

The discursive field of 'affect theory', when divorced from its materialist and anti-colonial imperatives, risks becoming another bourgeois academic exercise, a mere intellectual 'turn' devoid of revolutionary teeth. This artifice of neutrality is precisely the institutional language Edward Said excoriated in his analysis of Orientalism; a language designed to categorize, to exoticize, to contain. Affect, particularly the 'negative' affects — rage, grief, disgust — when manifested by the subaltern body, is immediately pathologized, categorized as irrational, or worse, weaponized against the subject in the service of the prevailing order.

The Western episteme, historically and presently dominated by white patriarchal institutions, legislates the permissible spectrum of emotion. For the racialized, female-presenting body, expressing anything beyond docile 'resilience' or palatable 'sadness' is an act of epistemic violence against the established emotional economy. This constitutes a direct link to what Frantz Fanon articulated as the 'zone of non-being,' where the colonized subject's interiority is systematically denied, their humanity contingent upon the settler's gaze. Our rage, then, is not merely an emotion; it is a fundamental challenge to this ontological negation.

EMOTIONAL LABOR AS CAPITALIST EXTRACTION

Consider the insidious mechanisms of emotional labor. bell hooks meticulously demonstrated how marginalized bodies, particularly women of color, are compelled to perform an endless, uncompensated affective servitude within patriarchal and capitalist frameworks. This is not merely 'niceness'; it is the systemic extraction of psychological energy, a constant demand for pacification, for empathy directed outwards, for the maintenance of oppressive social relations. This unpaid labor, often cloaked in the deceptive language of 'community building' or 'self-care' (a neoliberal absurdity), is fundamentally a form of value extraction, echoing Mbembe's framework of necropolitics, where certain lives are rendered useful only for their capacity to absorb and mediate the violence of others.
