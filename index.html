FANON'S DIGITAL WOUND

Frantz Fanon’s analysis of the psychopathology of colonialism finds its contemporary echo in the digital realm. The colonised subject, already fractured by physical dispossession, is now subjected to a parallel, pervasive psychic violence through controlled digital access, manipulated narratives, and constant surveillance. The 'native' body, digitally dispossessed, becomes an avatar of vulnerability. The very tools presented as 'connectivity' or 'development' – 4G networks, satellite imagery – are in fact sophisticated instruments of control, enforcing a digital apartheid where freedom of information is a privilege, not a right. The digital 'wall' is as potent as the concrete one, fostering a profound sense of alienation, of non-existence within the colonizer's networked world. The demand for digital self-determination is thus a direct continuation of the struggle for bodily autonomy.

MBEMBE AND THE NECROPOLITICS OF DATA

Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics – the power to dictate who may live and who must die, or be subjected to 'social death' – extends seamlessly into the digital sphere. Here, the biopolitical management of populations morphs into a necropolitical orchestration of data. Whose data is collected? Whose is ignored? Whose digital presence is amplified, and whose is systematically suppressed or erased? The algorithms of the global north determine the digital life and death of the global south, manifesting in shadow-banning, content suppression, and the systematic deplatforming of voices that challenge the hegemonic narrative. This is not mere censorship; it is a profound act of digital execution, rendering certain realities non-existent within the dominant informational ecology. The colonised body, once physically marked for death, is now digitally interred.

Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak's query, "Can the subaltern speak?", resonates with brutal clarity in the age of data colonialism.
