A DISMANTLING OF REPRESENTATIONAL VIOLENCE

The digital interface, this very screen, is not a neutral tableau. It is a contested archive, a site saturated with the unseen violences of representation. We speak of 'post'-colonialism, a dangerous temporal euphemism, as if the structures of power articulated by Edward Said in Orientalism have somehow dissipated. They have not. They have merely transmogrified, re-territorialized across algorithms and data streams, perpetuating the same discursive colonization.

>>> Mbembe. Necropolitics. The perpetual production of zones of death, of subjects rendered expendable through an epistemic triage. >>> This is not a metaphor. This is the material reality of the occupied, the dispossessed, whose very existence is framed as an aberration to the Western hegemonic project. >>> The "Orient" is not a geographic location. It is a project of power, an ideological construct designed for the self-validation and territorial acquisition of the metropole.

The gaze continues to define, to contain, to erase. From the ethnographic museum display to the curated news cycle, the subaltern, as Gayatri Spivak meticulously warns, still struggles to speak, trapped within the representational grids of the colonizer. When they do, their voice is often distorted, mediated, or outright silenced by the institutional mechanisms of liberal benevolence – which are, let us be clear, nothing more than a refined apparatus of control.

The Weaponization of Epistemic Hegemony

Fanon knew this intimately. The psychic scarring of colonialism is not merely historical; it is ontological. It permeates the very structures of knowing, dictating whose knowledge is legitimate, whose history is verifiable, whose suffering is permissible. The so-called academic discourse that seeks to 'understand both sides' is an act of epistemic violence, equalizing the oppressor and the oppressed, legitimizing the theft. This is the precise function of liberal moderation: to neutralize radical truth.
