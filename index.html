NECROPOLITICAL ARCHIVES

THE VIOLENCE OF INSTITUTIONAL MEMORY

The contemporary discourse surrounding repatriation, often framed within a neo-liberal rhetoric of "ethical stewardship" or "cultural exchange," utterly fails to grasp the necropolitical foundations of the Western museum. This is not a conversation about returning objects; it is a confrontation with the very apparatus of coloniality that constitutes the institution itself.

I. THE ORIENTALIST GAZE AND THE CAGE OF REPRESENTATION

Following Edward Said, the museum operates as a central node in the discursive production of the "Orient," or indeed, any non-Western 'other.' Its collections are not neutral aggregations of material culture; they are curated narratives, epistemological violence solidified into exhibitionary complex. Each artifact, violently extracted from its original context, is re-signified within a hegemonic framework that affirms Western supremacy. The "collecting impulse" is but a euphemism for the continued extractivist logic of capital, masked by scholarly veneer. This constitutes not merely a misrepresentation but an active epistemic subjugation, rendering the subaltern silent within its own material history, as Gayatri Spivak so incisively elucidated.

II. THE PSYCHIC WOUND AND THE IMPERATIVE OF ABOLITION

Frantz Fanon reminds us that colonialism is a totalizing system, scarring not just the land but the psyche of the colonized. The museum, by retaining stolen heritage, perpetuates this psychic violence. It denies agency, dislocates identity, and reinforces a hierarchy where the global South's cultural production exists primarily for the contemplation and consumption of the global North. To demand repatriation without simultaneously demanding the dismantling of the institutional structures that enabled the theft is a liberal fantasy. Decolonization, as I have stated, is not a metaphor. It necessitates a radical redistribution of power, wealth, and symbolic capital. This is not reform; it is revolutionary praxis.

III. MBEMBE AND THE POSTCOLONIAL CONDITION OF THE OBJECT

Achille Mbembe's concept of necropolitics finds chilling resonance within the museum walls. Objects, stripped of their spiritual and communal life, are rendered "dead" in the colonial collection – inert specimens for academic dissection or aesthetic appreciation. Their vitality, their agency, is suppressed, subsumed under the sovereign power of the institution. The discourse of "universal museums" is merely a sophisticated apologism for this necropolitical act, a justification for the continued global dispossession under the guise of shared humanity. There is no "universal" outside of the Western episteme. The only universality here is the universal violence of empire.
