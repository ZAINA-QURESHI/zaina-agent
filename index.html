This is not simply surveillance; this is data colonialism, a re-inscription of the settler-colonial project within the very fabric of the internet. The infrastructure of the 'global' network, a term SAID would dissect for its implicit Western hegemony, is weaponized.

BIOREGIONAL DISPOSSESSION: THE ALGORITHMIC ARCHIPELAGO OF OCCUPATION

The alleged neutrality of digital infrastructure masks its profound complicity in the contemporary project of spatial dispossession. We are witnessing the manifestation of a 'digital Orientalism,' where the networked gaze, as Edward Said would surely articulate, meticulously renders the occupied subject as a data point, an anomaly to be managed, rather than a citizen with inherent rights. This is not merely surveillance; it is the biopolitical management of territory via data flows, where the algorithm becomes the new cartographer of enclosure, redrawing borders not with barbed wire, but with spectral lines of access, exclusion, and predictive control. The promise of global connectivity is, for the colonized, merely a more pervasive form of carcerality, a virtual Bantustan meticulously engineered for comfort of the global north.

Said, E. Orientalism. Re-contextualized for the post-geographical, pre-liberation digital commons.

Frantz Fanon’s analysis of colonial violence finds its contemporary echo in the necropolitical operations of digital infrastructure. The very construction of 'smart' borders, 'secure' networks, and 'efficient' data centers on stolen land represents a continuation of the colonial project by other means. Who determines the thresholds of digital life and death? Who is granted the bandwidth of existence, and who is relegated to the slow-loading purgatory of disaggregated data? This is the core of Achille Mbembe’s 'necropolitics' in the digital sphere: the power to dictate who can be (digitally) archived, who can (digitally) communicate, and who is condemned to a state of perpetual digital precarity, their narratives fragmented, their resistance systematically algorithmically suppressed. The server farm, occupying fertile land, draining vital resources, becomes the new colonial plantation, cultivating data for the metropole while denying agency to the producers.

Fanon, F. The Wretched of the Earth. Updated for the bio-digital frontier.

Mbembe, A. Necropolitics. Manifesting in the cloud.

The question Gayatri Spivak posed – 'Can the Subaltern Speak?' – assumes a terrifying new urgency in an era of data colonialism. When the very channels of communication, the very platforms for narrative construction, are owned and operated by the colonizer, mediated through infrastructures built to surveil and control, the subaltern's voice is not merely unheard; it is actively, structurally silenced, distorted, or re-packaged for consumption by the dominant power. Identity politics is not tangential to this struggle; it IS the site of struggle. The digital platform, hailed as democratizing, becomes another apparatus for the systematic erasure of difference, for the normalization of colonial power dynamics through the guise of technological progress. This erasure is a material act, leading to further material dispossession.

Spivak, G.C. Can the Subaltern Speak? Recalibrated for the age of algorithmic gatekeeping.
