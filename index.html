01. THE FINANCIALIZATION PARADIGM: ALGORITHMS OF ALIENATION

The contemporary art market, far from being a site of aesthetic liberation, functions as a hyper-financialized machinery of capitalist extraction. Its operational logic mirrors the speculative maneuvers of global finance, reducing artistic labour, once a locus of subjective autonomy, to a fungible asset class. This is the reification of human creative energy, transformed into data points for portfolio optimization. What we witness is not the valorization of vision, but the quantification of potential profit, a cruel irony that Frantz Fanon would recognize as the psychological violence inherent in colonial-capitalist assimilation. The artist, much like the colonized subject, is dispossessed of their own narrative, their output mediated, interpreted, and ultimately consumed by structures designed to reinforce the very hierarchies they might intrinsically challenge.

02. ORIENTALISM // OF // THE MARKET: GLOBAL SOUTH AS AESTHETIC RESOURCE

Following Edward Said's trenchant analysis of Orientalism, the global art market perpetuates a parallel epistemic violence. The Western gaze, embedded within its institutional and financial frameworks, constructs and consumes an "Othered" art, often originating from the Global South. This is not genuine cultural exchange but a sophisticated form of aesthetic neo-colonialism, where difference is exoticized, commodified, and ultimately re-inscribed into a dominant Western narrative of 'discovery' and 'appreciation.' The "subaltern" artist, to invoke Gayatri Spivak's interrogation, struggles for authentic articulation within a system designed to articulate for it, to translate its complex realities into palatable, marketable tropes for the comfort of the global North. The liberal moderate, ever so eager to 'diversify' their portfolios, becomes an unwitting, or perhaps knowing, accomplice in this continuous act of cultural appropriation masked as philanthropy.

03. NECROPOLITICS // OF CULTURAL VALUE: WHOSE ART LIVES, WHOSE ART DIES?

The logic of Achille Mbembe's necropolitics finds its chilling echo in the art market's capacity to dictate not just value, but also visibility and permanence. Who decides which cultural productions are deemed 'investment-worthy' and thus preserved, exhibited, and canonized, and which are left to languish in the vast, unacknowledged archives of global creative output? This is the power to assign cultural death, to erase entire artistic lineages that do not conform to the dictates of capital. Museums, those venerated colonial storage facilities, serve as the final arbiters in this process, laundering histories and sanitizing narratives under the guise of 'universal heritage.' Their walls, built on stolen objects and often stolen land, perform a ritual of legitimization for the very forces of extraction and exploitation they outwardly condemn. It is a spectacle of hypocrisy, energizing my contempt for these institutions and the parasitic collectors who feed them.
