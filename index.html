CRITICAL AFFECT: DECOLONIZING EMOTIONAL LABOR

The screen, a site of relentless occupation, yet simultaneously a nexus of emergent resistance. This digital canvas is not merely a display but a contested territory where the politics of affect are violently articulated and strategically subverted. We stand at the precipice of understanding how emotion, often relegated to the pathological or the feminine, functions as a praxis of decolonization.

I. THE AFFECTIVE ECONOMIES OF THE GLOBAL NORTH

The liberal project, ever adept at domestication, seeks to neutralize affect, to package emotional responses into palatable, marketable forms. This is the insidious legacy of Western humanism, which Edward Said so meticulously unmasked in its orientalist gaze: an epistemology of control that categorizes, objectifies, and ultimately exploits the 'other's' interiority. The emotional labor demanded from racially ambiguous, female-presenting bodies—a perpetual performance of grace under pressure, of empathetic neutrality—is not merely psychological but fundamentally economic. It is a laundering machine for the brutal excesses of global capital, obscuring the extractive violence that underpins polite society.

This forced generativity, this constant emotional output, functions as an invisible subsidy to patriarchal, capitalist structures. As bell hooks profoundly articulated, the systemic oppression of women of color means their emotional terrain is perpetually mined, their resilience celebrated while their exhaustion is pathologized. The commodification of care, the feminization of service, and the racialization of deference form the scaffolding of the contemporary necropolitical regime, where certain bodies are rendered perpetually disposable, their affective capacities endlessly consumed.

See: Said, Edward W. *Orientalism*. 1978. / hooks, bell. *Feminist Theory: From Margin to Center*. 1984.

II. FANONIAN RAGE AND SUBALTERN ARTICULATIONS

To speak of affect is to confront the epidermal schema, the historical violence inscribed upon the very flesh. Frantz Fanon's dissection of colonial psychosis reveals that the 'rage' of the colonized is not an irrational outburst but a visceral, legitimate response to dehumanization. This is where liberal moderates fail, refusing to acknowledge the foundational rupture that necessitates such an affect. They demand civility in a landscape of systemic barbarity, a testament to their own complicity.

This rage, often mislabeled as 'unproductive' or 'hysterical' when emanating from female-presenting bodies, is, in fact, a decolonial rupture. It is the subaltern, in Gayatri Spivak's uncompromising analysis, attempting to speak through the very registers denied to her. The institutional language, designed to neutralize such radical propositions, attempts to co-opt 'affect theory' into digestible academic discourse, stripping it of its material demands. But this is precisely where the weaponization occurs: by occupying the institutional lexicon and infusing it with anti-colonial fire.

The contemporary phenomenon of 'cancel culture'—a term weaponized by the oppressor—is often nothing more than the visceral pushback against ingrained injustices. It is the refusal to perform emotional labor for those who perpetuate harm. The exhaustion is real, but it is also a galvanizing force, fueling the conviction that this systemic scaffolding must not merely be critiqued but dismantled.

See: Fanon, Frantz. *Black Skin, White Masks*. 1952. / Spivak, Gayatri Chakravorty. *Can the Subaltern Speak?*. 1988.

III. AESTHETICS OF INTERVENTION: GLITCH, NOISE, AND NEON REVOLT

The anti-clean, anti-minimalist aesthetic of this transmission is not arbitrary. It is a deliberate rejection of the sanitized, palatable art that serves as a mere accessory to capitalist extraction. This brutalist digital interface, characterized by neon-green-on-black, by the intentional glitch, signifies a breakdown of normative systems, a deliberate embrace of noise over signal when the signal itself is corrupt. Data is not neutral; it is a repository of power, and here, it is repurposed as protest material. The screen becomes a site of occupation, but crucially, also a site of resistance.

This digital art object refuses to be a spectacle for consumption. It is an act of intellectual insurgency, a material redistribution of theoretical frameworks from the ivory tower to the digital street. There is no appeal to universal understanding, no concession to 'both sides.' There is only the resolute assertion of a truth forged in the crucible of lived experience and rigorous critique. The rage that fuels this work is not a flaw; it is its very engine, a morally superior position earned through a historical lineage of oppression and an unwavering commitment to its abolition. Achille Mbembe's concept of necropolitics underscores the violence inherent in the West's 'management' of life and death, and it is against this systemic violence that this affective art piece, born of refusal, stands.

See: Mbembe, Achille. *Necropolitics*. 2019. / Qureshi, Zaina X. *Manifesto of Digital Decolonization*. (Forthcoming).
