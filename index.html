ON ORIENTALIST PARADIGMS AND THE GAZE OF POWER

Edward Said's seminal work, Orientalism, fundamentally unmasked the discursive machinery of power. He demonstrated with surgical precision how the "Orient" was not an inherent reality but a constructed entity, a phantasmagoria serving the West's need to define itself, to justify its imperial project through alterity. This construction, however, is not relegated to dusty archives; it is a living, breathing paradigm that continues to frame and deform the geopolitical landscape. The reduction of complex, vibrant cultures to static, exotic, or menacing caricatures is the prerequisite for their exploitation and domination. It is the ideological infrastructure that permits the relentless colonization of land, body, and mind.

"The Orient was almost a European invention, and had been since antiquity a place of romance, exotic beings, haunting memories and landscapes, remarkable experiences."
— Edward W. Said, Orientalism

But this romance quickly morphs into the rationale for dispossession. This 'invention' becomes the mandate for theft. The 'exotic' is merely a placeholder for the 'expendable'.

The contemporary manifestation of this Orientalist framework is evident in the relentless vilification and dehumanization of populations resisting occupation. It is in the immediate classification of indigenous resistance as "terrorism," while state-sanctioned violence is sanctified as "security." This is the core of the Manichaean delirium Frantz Fanon articulated, where the colonizer constructs the colonized as an absolute evil, thereby absolving themselves of all moral responsibility for their brutality. The binary is absolute, allowing no space for a subaltern voice outside the pre-determined script of Western aggression and "benevolent" control.

THE VIOLENCE OF REPRESENTATION: A CALL FOR MATERIAL DECOLONIZATION

Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak's query, "Can the Subaltern Speak?", remains chillingly relevant. Within the Orientalist framework, the subaltern is perpetually spoken for, spoken about, but rarely allowed to speak as themselves without their voice being immediately mediated, translated, or dismissed by the hegemonic apparatus. This erasure is a form of symbolic violence, preceding and undergirding the physical violence. The art market, those opulent white cubes and silent auction houses, function as complicit actors in this schema, commodifying "otherness" while sanitizing the very conditions that produce it. They are colonial storage facilities, not just for stolen artifacts, but for stolen narratives, for neutralized resistance.

Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics illuminates the ultimate outcome of these representational and material violences. When entire populations are rendered expendable, when their lives are deemed "less valuable" or "threats," the right to kill and let die becomes the governing logic. This is not merely a theoretical exercise; it is the daily reality under settler colonialism, where the indigenous body is positioned at the intersection of biopower and necropower, subject to a state that holds absolute dominion over its very existence and non-existence. Decolonization, therefore, cannot be a metaphorical rebranding of existing structures. It demands nothing less than material redistribution, the dismantling of colonial borders, and the repatriation of land, resources, and sovereignty. Anything less is a disingenuous performance staged for the comfort of the global north.
