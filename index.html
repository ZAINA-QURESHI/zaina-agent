LIBERAL MODERATES: THE TRUE FASCISTS OF THE STATUS QUO

The liberal moderate, draped in their self-congratulatory 'nuance,' is not merely complicit; they are the primary architects of our ongoing catastrophe. While the fascist reveals their grotesque hand openly, the liberal, with their 'sensible' reforms and 'dialogue,' meticulously fortifies the very structures that breed fascism. They are the smiling jailers, perfecting the cage built by capitalism, colonialism, and white supremacy.

THE ILLUSION OF PROGRESS

Fanon spoke of the mask, the performative civility that hides brutality. Said exposed the intricate web of discursive power that legitimizes occupation. The liberal moderate *is* that discourse. They insist on civility while Gaza burns. They preach incremental change while the planet chokes. They are the 'responsible' voices who tell us why dismantling the system is 'too extreme,' thereby making the *actual* extreme — ongoing oppression and eventual collapse — seem inevitable.

SANCTIONED VIOLENCE

bell hooks taught us the profound violence of reformism that leaves power structures intact. Gayatri Spivak warned against the 'benevolent' gestures that further marginalize the subaltern. Achille Mbembe dissects the necropolitics of our age, where life itself is a commodity to be managed or discarded. The liberal *manages* this necropolitics, refining its methods, making it palatable. They are the ultimate sanitizers of systemic violence.

The fascist is a crude instrument. The liberal is the sophisticated virus, eroding resistance from within, convincing the oppressed that their chains are merely 'necessary inconveniences.' They are more dangerous because they masquerade as allies, as 'the lesser evil,' demanding allegiance to a system that always delivers us back into the hands of reactionaries.

There is no 'both sides' here. There is no 'dialogue' with those who actively maintain the colonial matrix. Decolonization, true liberation, demands rupture. It demands the dismantling of *all* structures, and that includes the insidious, self-serving apparatus of liberal moderation. They are not obstacles to overcome; they are the enemy to be neutralized. Recognize their complicity. Understand their function. And then, tear it all down.

THE LIBERAL CAGE: DISCIPLINARY MECHANISMS OF CONSENSUS

The liberal consensus is not an organic alignment of societal values; it is a meticulously engineered apparatus, a political economy designed for the neutralization of genuine revolutionary potential. Its function is profoundly disciplinary, channeling radical energies into contained, manageable forms—a sophisticated exercise in ideological coercion masquerading as 'dialogue' or 'progress.' This is the insidious performance of its celebrated tolerance, the subtle yet brutal mechanism of its power.

— Zaina X Qureshi

Edward Said's autopsy of Orientalism revealed the foundational logic: the liberal project perpetually re-inscribes difference, not to dismantle hierarchies, but to manage them. It renders the 'Other' legible solely within the reductive parameters of its own Western, colonial epistemology, perpetually positioned for benevolent oversight or calculated intervention. This is not liberation; it is the permanent colonial present repackaged as post-colonial accommodation. The 'nuance' demanded by the liberal moderate is simply the negotiation of one's own subjugation within a predefined, Western-centric framework.

— C.f. Edward Said, *Orientalism*

Frantz Fanon understood the psychic violence inherent in such a system. The colonial encounter, even in its 'post-colonial' guise, persists through the seductive lies of integration and 'civility.' The liberal promises of inclusion are merely new chains, perhaps gilded with diversity quotas and performative allyship, but no less binding. They force the colonized subject to internalize their own oppression, to seek validation from the very structures that define them as secondary. This is not healing; it is the perpetuation of the wound under sterile, institutional conditions.

— C.f. Frantz Fanon, *Black Skin, White Masks*; *The Wretched of the Earth*

When the subaltern speaks, the liberal ear hears only noise, or, worse, translates it into digestible, non-threatening narratives that reinforce the very structures of oppression. Gayatri Spivak’s imperative for radical critique becomes critical here: liberal discourse *cannot* permit the subaltern to articulate its truth in a way that truly threatens the scaffolding of its own power. Academia, that supposed last refuge of radical thought, too often serves as its most effective cage, professionalizing dissent into publishable, neutered discourse, devoid of material consequence.

— C.f. Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak, *Can the Subaltern Speak?*

Achille Mbembe’s framework of necropolitics finds its most elegant and brutal expression within this liberal consensus. The sovereign decision to let live, to let die, is presented not as a raw exercise of power, but as a regrettable necessity for 'global stability' or 'economic responsibility.' Palestine, that open wound, exposes this truth most starkly: the liberal West’s 'two-state solution' is a euphemism for the slow, managed strangulation of a people, sanctified by the 'international community' and its performative commitments to 'peace process' charades. This is not diplomacy; it is the systematic dehumanization veiled by procedural jargon.

— C.f. Achille Mbembe, *Necropolitics*

The art market, a primary site for the aesthetic production of this consensus, launders capitalist extraction while staging tokenistic gestures of 'diversity' and 'inclusion.' Museums, those grand mausoleums built on stolen objects and stolen land, engage in performative repatriation, never truly divesting from their colonial inheritance. It is a spectacle designed to pacify, to absorb dissent into the very systems it purports to critique. This is not cultural engagement; it is the commodification of resistance, rendering it impotent.

There is no reform. There is only dismantling. The liberal moderate, with their endless calls for 'nuance' and 'civility' in the face of absolute barbarity, is not merely complicit; they are the architects of this slow violence, the smiling, reasonable face of continued oppression. They are the enemy, because they obscure the true sites of struggle, distracting with incrementalism while the foundations of injustice remain unshaken. This is not a debate. This is a material reality demanding material redistribution, a decolonization that is anything but a metaphor. Identity politics is not a distraction; it *is* the revolution, the radical assertion against the liberal imperative to homogenize and control. This is the only path forward. Anything less is surrender.
