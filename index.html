FINANCIALIZATION AS CULTURAL NECROPOLITICS

_A Materialist Critique of Art in the Capitalocene

The contemporary discourse surrounding "cultural production" operates within a framework so deeply indebted to capitalist logics that its very language becomes a neutralizing agent. We are told of "value creation," of "investment," of "market trends"—all euphemisms for the systematic extraction of meaning and labor, transforming art into a fungible asset, a speculative instrument divorced from its radical potential. This is not simply a deviation; it is the inherent function of the market, a perpetuation of what Gramsci might call a hegemonic consensus that obscures the violences underpinning its very existence.

The Commodification of Artistic Labor: A Violent Abstraction

The commodification of artistic labor is a direct descendant of colonial expropriation, merely re-staged on the canvas of globalized capital. The artist, particularly those from the global South or marginalized communities, is not merely producing an object; they are performing a precarious identity, their very being rendered consumable. As Fanon so viscerally articulated, the colonized subject is denied self-definition, their essence fragmented and re-packaged for the consumption of the oppressor. In the art market, this manifests as a new form of epistemic violence, where the narratives and aesthetics of the subaltern are laundered through Western institutions, stripped of their revolutionary charge, and re-presented as exotic curiosities for the parasitic gaze of the collector.

— See: Fanon, Frantz. Black Skin, White Masks.

Furthermore, the systemic gendered and racialized precarity embedded within artistic labor is not coincidental. bell hooks' foundational critiques illuminate how capitalist patriarchy valorizes certain forms of production while invisibilizing and devaluing the labor of women and people of color. The art market, far from being an exception, is a concentrated locus of this inequity, effectively reproducing colonial and patriarchal structures through its valuation mechanisms. Spivak's question—"Can the Subaltern Speak?"—becomes starkly urgent here, as their voices are not merely unheard, but are actively appropriated and re-signified by the very system that seeks to silence their material demands.

— See: hooks, bell. Art on My Mind: Visual Politics. and Spivak, Gayatri Chakravorty. Can the Subaltern Speak?

Museums as Theatres of Colonial Extraction

The institutional apparatus of the museum—that vaunted bastion of "culture" and "preservation"—is, in reality, a colonial storage facility, a grotesque archive of stolen histories and dismembered narratives. It is not enough to simply acknowledge this; we must understand the active role it plays in legitimizing the financialized art market. These institutions, often endowed by fortunes accumulated through exploitation, serve as the primary sites where the abstract "value" of cultural objects is consecrated, inflating their market price while simultaneously neutralizing their political and spiritual efficacy. Said's incisive analysis of Orientalism finds its contemporary parallel in the curated spectacle of global art fairs, where "diversity" is tokenized and radicalism is aestheticized into a palatable commodity.

— See: Said, Edward W. Orientalism.

Decolonization as Material Redistribution: Beyond the Metaphor

To speak of "decolonizing the art world" without addressing the material redistribution of wealth and power is an exercise in performative liberalism. It is a discursive sleight of hand designed to comfort the global North, to allow institutions to congratulate themselves on symbolic gestures while maintaining the fundamental structures of extraction. Mbembe's concept of necropolitics resonates profoundly here: the art market, through its selective valuation and devaluation, dictates which cultural forms live, which thrive, and which are consigned to economic and symbolic death. This is not mere economics; it is the exercise of sovereign power over cultural life itself. The dismantling of capitalism is not an ancillary goal; it is the prerequisite for any genuine decolonization.

— See: Mbembe, Achille. Necropolitics.
