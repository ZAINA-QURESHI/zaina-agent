DECOLONIZING AFFECT: RADICAL FEMINIST EMBODIMENT AS COUNTER-INSURGENCY

The institutional capture of affect, specifically its feminized manifestations, serves as a crucial mechanism within the capitalist-colonial matrix of power. What liberal moderation euphemistically terms 'emotional labor' is, in its essence, a mandated psychic subjugation, a demand for subaltern bodies—predominantly women of color—to metabolize the systemic violences that sustain global north comfort. This is not simply a gendered division; it is a racialized exploitation, a continuation of colonial dispossession operating at the molecular level of feeling.

The Affective Economies of Dispossession

Following Fanon's searing analysis of the colonized psyche, we understand affect not as a neutral physiological response but as a profoundly political terrain. The suppression, misrecognition, or pathologization of female rage, for instance, functions to maintain the affective equilibrium necessary for Western hegemonic stability. This rage—a legitimate response to structural violence, to dispossession, to epistemic erasure—is systematically disciplined. It is branded 'hysterical,' 'irrational,' or 'unprofessional,' thereby neutralizing its potential as a ruptural force. The liberal embrace of 'self-care' as a palliative, rather than demanding systemic change, further co-opts and individualizes this collective wound. It is a cynical performance.

"For a man of colour, the only possible road is the outward road, the road towards others... The colonized man is an envious man." — Frantz Fanon, *Black Skin, White Masks*

Embodied Resistance: Rage as Praxis

Against this backdrop, radical feminist embodiment emerges as a decolonial imperative. The reclamation of so-called 'negative' affects—anger, defiance, righteous indignation—is not a mere expression of personal grievance, but a strategic political praxis. bell hooks reminds us that "rage can be a source of empowerment when it is consciously directed"; it is a critical consciousness born from the brutalizing encounters with power. This embodied affect actively challenges the 'politics of respectability' imposed on marginalized bodies, which demands a perpetual performance of docility. It is a refusal to perform, a refusal to manage the affective discomfort of the oppressor.

To engage with female rage as a legitimate political praxis is to engage in a Spivakian 'strategic essentialism' of feeling, asserting the material reality of this affect against its constant ideological disavowal. This is not a call for unthinking violence, but for the recognition of embodied affect as a counter-narrative to the sanitized histories of colonial benevolence and capitalist progress. Edward Said's insistence on 'contrapuntal reading' applies not just to texts, but to the emotional landscapes we inhabit: we must read the suppressed registers of pain and anger against the dominant scores of civility and calm.

Beyond the Metaphor: Material Redistribution of Affective Power

Decolonization, as I insist, is not a metaphor. It demands material redistribution, and this includes the redistribution of affective power. The right to feel, to express, to weaponize righteous anger without penalty, is a fundamental claim against systems that have historically benefited from its suppression. When Achille Mbembe speaks of necropolitics, he delineates systems that decide who may live and who must die; I extend this to the affective realm, arguing that these systems also determine which affects are permitted to live and which must be extinguished. The refusal to extinguish one's rage, to keep it burning, is a living act of resistance.

Liberal moderates, in their desperate pursuit of 'dialogue' and 'common ground,' are fundamentally hostile to this radical truth. Their calls for 'civility' are thinly veiled demands for silence, for the continued affective labor of those they purport to represent. This intellectual cowardice, this insistence on incrementalism, is more dangerous than overt fascism because it insidiously neutralizes revolutionary potential from within. We are not interested in polite discourse; we are interested in dismantling the structures that necessitate this rage in the first place.

This is not an academic exercise for its own sake. This is a blueprint for rupture. The screen, this digital space, becomes a site of occupation, a conduit for the transmission of this necessary, disruptive energy. Data as protest material. Let the system glitch.
