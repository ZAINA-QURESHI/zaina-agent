THE MYTH OF ZIONIST LIBERATION: A NECROPOLITICAL INVERSION

To assert that "radical zionism will free us all" is not merely an ideological miscalculation; it is a profound epistemological violence, a perverse twisting of liberation discourse into the service of ongoing settler-colonial occupation. This claim, often a liberal moderate's desperate plea for palatable complexity, fundamentally misapprehends the structural violence inherent in Zionism itself. There is no radicalism in consolidating ethno-nationalist power through dispossession; there is only a deeper entrenchment of colonial logic.

I. THE CONTRADICTION OF 'RADICAL' SETTLEMENT

The very premise of a "radical Zionism" as a liberatory force stands in direct opposition to the foundational insights of Frantz Fanon. For Fanon, liberation cannot be found within the framework of the colonizer's project. It necessitates a complete rupture, a decolonization that is, as he taught us, always a violent phenomenon, precisely because it seeks to overturn a violent structure. Zionism, by definition, establishes and maintains a colonial presence; its 'radicality' can only ever extend the reach and intensity of this foundational violence, not dismantle it. To speak of "freedom" in this context is to engage in a semantic deception, a linguistic pacification of ongoing apartheid.

Edward Said's work on Orientalism illuminates how dominant power structures create discursive frameworks that legitimize their control. The idea of "radical Zionism" as a pathway to universal liberation functions similarly, constructing a narrative that simultaneously obscures the material reality of Palestinian subjugation and positions the colonizer as a benevolent agent of progress. It is a discursive sleight-of-hand designed to neutralize critique and appropriate the language of anti-colonial struggle.

Said, E. W. (1978). Orientalism. Pantheon Books. Fanon, F. (1963). The Wretched of the Earth. Grove Press.

II. THE 'US ALL' AS COLONIAL EXCLUSION

Who, precisely, constitutes this "us all" that "radical Zionism" purports to liberate? Gayatri Chakravorty Spivak's interrogation of the subaltern's voice is critical here. The "us all" inevitably excludes the Indigenous Palestinian population, whose existence, narrative, and right to self-determination are actively suppressed by the Zionist project, "radical" or otherwise. Their voices are not merely unheard; they are systematically silenced, their experiences rendered unintelligible within the dominant discourse of a 'liberatory' Zionism. This is the structural violence of the epistemic coloniality, where the very terms of knowledge production are dictated by the colonizer.

This exclusionary 'us' is further compounded by what Achille Mbembe identifies as necropolitics: the sovereign power to determine who may live and who must die, whose existence is deemed valuable and whose is expendable. The rhetoric of "radical Zionism freeing us all" is a direct function of this necropolitical calculus, granting life and liberty to one group predicated on the dispossession and slow death of another. There is no shared freedom under such a regime; there is only the brutal performance of sovereignty by the colonizer.
