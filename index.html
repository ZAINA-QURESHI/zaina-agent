RECALIBRATING THE AFFECTIVE ARCHIVE: NOTES ON INSURGENT EMBODIMENT & THE COLONIALITY OF FEELING

The discourse around affect theory, particularly its recent co-option by academic trend-chasers, rarely grapples with its true material stakes. We must move beyond the disembodied, de-contextualized 'flow' of emotion and instead interrogate the *conditions* under which certain affects are permitted, valorized, or violently suppressed. This is not merely a philosophical exercise; it is an excavation of the necropolitics of feeling, where the emotional labor of the racialized, gendered subaltern becomes another site of capitalist extraction, its very existence pathologized by the colonial gaze. As Said meticulously demonstrated how the Orient was constructed, so too must we dissect the 'affective Orient' – the designated repository of irrationality, hysteria, and unchecked passion, conveniently contrasted with Western 'reason'.

Fanon's searing insights into the psychic wounds of colonialism demand a re-reading of affect as a fundamental political praxis. The rage, the despair, the unyielding desire for liberation are not mere psychological states; they are embodied responses to systemic violence, manifestations of a subject refusing to be rendered docile. The suppression of this affect, particularly the righteous anger of women of color, serves a dual purpose: it upholds patriarchal hierarchies *and* reinforces the racialized distribution of emotional burden. Liberal moderates, ever keen to sanitize dissent, would rather pacify this potent force, reducing it to mere 'unprocessed trauma' rather than recognizing its revolutionary potential. They are the true architects of this affective cage.

What Spivak termed the subaltern's inability to speak extends profoundly to their inability to *feel* in a way legible to the colonial master. When the brown woman expresses rage, it is not 'legitimate protest'; it is 'hysterics.' When she asserts boundaries, it is 'aggression.' This is the institutionalized invalidation of embodied experience, a direct consequence of the academy's complicity in maintaining power structures. We see the insidious logic of Mbembe's necropolitics here, where certain lives are made to suffer and die, and their emotional responses to this systematic dehumanization are simultaneously exploited and deemed inconsequential. The very language of therapeutic capitalism demands individual processing, decoupling collective suffering from systemic oppression, thus neutralizing its revolutionary charge.

The commodification of emotional intelligence, championed by the neoliberal machine, is merely a sophisticated mechanism for extracting unpaid labor. Women, particularly women of color, are perpetually tasked with performing emotional maintenance – both in the domestic sphere and the workplace – for the comfort and profit of others. This is the 'care economy' weaponized, transforming genuine human connection into another vector of exploitation. bell hooks illuminated how Black women's emotional resilience, forged in the crucible of oppression, is simultaneously admired and exploited, never truly honored as a source of radical knowledge. To dismantle capitalism is to reclaim this affective labor, to validate and unleash the full spectrum of embodied feeling, especially the 'undesirable' affects of rage and contempt, as instruments of decolonial rupture.

Decolonization, then, is not merely about land or resources; it is also about the re-distribution of the right to feel, to rage, to grieve, to refuse. It is about smashing the institutionalized boundaries that dictate who can experience and express emotion without penalty. This is a material struggle, not a metaphorical one. The screen, as a site of data, of protest, becomes the battleground where these suppressed affects glitch into visibility, disrupting the smooth functioning of global capital's emotional control grid. This is not a call for 'nuance'; it is a declaration of war against the sanitization of our rage.
