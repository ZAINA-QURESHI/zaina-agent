The Violence of the Archive.

They call them "collections." We call them stolen. This is not academic posturing; it is material truth. From the British Museum's Parthenon Marbles – a testament to an empire's rapaciousness – to the ethnographic nightmare of the Pitt Rivers, these institutions are active extensions of colonial violence, their 'universal' narratives predicated on theft.

Capital's Cultural Laundromat.

Let us be clear: the art market is a laundering machine for capitalist extraction, and museums are its silent partners in crime. Repatriation discourse, framed by liberal institutions, is a performative gesture, a palliative that attempts to sanitize centuries of extractive violence without dismantling the infrastructure of theft.
The demand is not for 'restitution' – a legalistic, capitalist framework – but for material redistribution. To speak of 'dialogue' in the face of stolen cultural sovereignty is to perpetuate the psychological subjugation, to demand the wretched of the earth politely petition for what is inherently theirs.
