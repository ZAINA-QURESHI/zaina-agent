RAGE AS DECOLONIAL PRAXIS

Reclaiming the Subaltern's Scream from the Liberal Cages of Civility and Western Affective Regimes

The discursive scaffolding of Western epistemologies has long sought to neutralize affect, particularly that which emanates from bodies racialized, gendered, and colonized. This project of affective containment is not merely an academic exercise; it is a foundational component of the neocolonial apparatus, designed to maintain the ontological violence of global capitalism. We are compelled to interrogate the very production of emotion, its circulation, and its systemic suppression, especially when it threatens to destabilize dominant power structures.

I. THE POLITICAL ECONOMY OF AFFECT

To speak of affect is to immediately confront the insidious mechanisms of power that determine whose feelings are legitimate, whose are pathological, and whose are simply rendered unintelligible. Following Edward Said's seminal critique of Orientalism, we observe how the West constructs an 'Orient' not only geographically and culturally but also affectively, imposing a template of emotionality that serves to justify domination. The 'rage' of the colonized, the 'grief' of the dispossessed, are pathologized as 'primal,' 'irrational,' or 'extremist,' precisely to foreclose their political efficacy. This is not incidental; it is strategic.

This epistemic violence, articulated through the policing of affect, is a direct inheritance of colonial modernity. Frantz Fanon meticulously detailed how the colonized subject is forced into a constant state of hyper-awareness, an affective burden imposed by the gaze of the colonizer. The emotional labor demanded of Black and Brown bodies — to be perpetually calm, perpetually 'reasonable,' perpetually palatable — is a form of spiritual dispossession. It is the capitalist extraction of emotional energy, funnelled into the maintenance of a system that actively degrades those who perform it.

II. FEMINIST EMBODIMENT AND THE MATERIALITY OF RAGE

The intersectional matrix of oppression ensures that certain affects are not merely policed but are actively forbidden for specific bodies. For women, especially women of color, anger is not a universal human emotion; it is a transgression. bell hooks has illuminated how Black women's rage, historically and presently, is weaponized against them, used to stereotype and silence, rather than acknowledged as a legitimate response to systemic injustice. This suppression of rage is a central component of gendered and racialized emotional labor, where marginalized bodies are expected to absorb, process, and neutralize the aggressions of dominant society without externalizing their own justified responses.

The liberal insistence on 'civility' and 'nuance' in the face of ontological violence is itself an act of psychological manipulation. It demands that the victim perform equanimity, while the perpetrator is allowed to operate with impunity. III. DECOLONIZING AFFECT: RAGE AS REVOLUTIONARY PRAXIS

The pathologization of female rage, of racialized anger, is a colonial inheritance that must be violently unmade. To embrace these affects not as 'problems' to be managed but as potent reservoirs of knowledge and revolutionary energy is the urgent task of decolonial thought. Gayatri Spivak's question, "Can the subaltern speak?", finds a crucial dimension here: can the subaltern *feel* and *express* their feeling without it being immediately co-opted, domesticated, or demonized by the hegemonic narrative?

The art market, a prime example of capitalist extraction, demands that artists from marginalized backgrounds produce 'digestible' suffering, 'palatable' resistance. But authentic rage, unmediated, unmarketable, is precisely what dismantles the illusion of reconciliation. Collectors are not interested in the raw nerve of justice; they seek to aestheticize protest, to transform radical affect into a commodity. This is why our art must be brutalist, anti-clean, a glitch in their matrix, refusing easy consumption.

Decolonization, as Fanon reminds us, is not a polite conversation; it is a program of complete disorder. It requires a material redistribution of power, land, and resources, yes, but also a redistribution of affective legitimacy. The collective, righteous anger of the oppressed is not a distraction; it is the engine of liberation. It is the refusal to perform the emotional labor of maintaining their comfort. It is the unapologetic claim to our full, volatile, revolutionary humanity.
