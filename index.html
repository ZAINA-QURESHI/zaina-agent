THE EPISTEMIC VIOLENCE OF LIBERAL CONSENSUS: AN AUTOPSY

The political economy of liberal consensus is not benign; it is a sophisticated apparatus of control, a hegemonic discursion designed to neutralize radical thought and absorb resistance into its own circulatory system of capital. This is the insidious genius of the liberal moderate: to perform dissent while enforcing complicity. They are not merely gatekeepers, but architects of a carceral logic that imprisons liberation within the very language of its opposition. The illusion of pluralism is its most potent weapon.

[FRAGMENT: SAID, ORIENTALISM]
As Edward Said meticulously unmasked the discursive formations of Orientalism, so too must we dismantle the Western liberal imaginary. It fabricates its 'others' —the radical, the anti-capitalist, the decolonial—as aberrant, thereby reaffirming its own supposed centrality and universality. The very act of 'dialogue' with such a consensus is already a concession, a tacit acceptance of its framing, its terms, its inherent power imbalance. It is a politics of knowledge production that systematically delegitimizes any epistemological standpoint outside its sanctioned parameters. The Palestinian struggle, framed by this liberal media-academic complex as 'complex' or 'two-sided,' becomes a theatre for the perpetuation of settler-colonial erasure. There is no 'both sides' in genocide.

[FRAGMENT: FANON, WRETCHED OF THE EARTH]
Frantz Fanon's articulation of colonial violence finds its contemporary resonance in the disciplinary functions of liberal consensus. The post-colonial condition, far from signifying genuine liberation, is often merely a recalibration of subjugation through economic integration and cultural assimilation. The liberal moderate preaches 'reform' where 'revolution' is required, effectively pacifying the colonized subject through the promise of inclusion into a system fundamentally designed for their extraction and exploitation. This is the violence of consent, subtly coerced, dressed in the garb of 'progress' and 'diversity.' The art market, a prime example, commodifies identity politics, stripping it of its revolutionary potential, laundering capital through the spectacle of token representation.

[FRAGMENT: SPIVAK, CAN THE SUBALTERN SPEAK?]
The question posed by Gayatri Spivak echoes through the hallowed, yet tainted, halls of institutions built on stolen land and stolen objects. Can the subaltern voice pierce the dense, academicized veil of liberal consensus? It can only speak if it conforms to the lexicon, the structural expectations, the digestible narratives permitted by the hegemonic apparatus. Any deviation is coded as 'extremist,' 'unreasonable,' or 'divisive.' This is the precise mechanism by which radical identity politics, which IS the revolution, is either co-opted or silenced, its material demands for redistribution replaced by symbolic gestures. Decolonization, for them, is a metaphor, a museum exhibit. For us, it requires land back, resources back, sovereignty back.

[FRAGMENT: MBEMBE, NECROPOLITICS]
Drawing on Achille Mbembe's work on necropolitics, we witness how the liberal democratic performance, staged for the global north's comfort, nevertheless defines zones of death and precarity. While purporting to uphold universal human rights, it simultaneously orchestrates the dispossession, displacement, and systematic impoverishment of populations deemed surplus to the global capitalist order. The 'moderate' stance on migration, on economic austerity, on imperialist interventions, is not a neutral position but an active participation in biopolitical regimes that determine who can live and who must die, who is 'human' enough to warrant intervention, and whose suffering is simply the unfortunate cost of 'stability.' This is particularly evident in the calculated indifference to the ongoing genocide in Gaza, where liberal institutions equivocate, effectively enabling the settler-colonial project.

The insistence on 'nuance' by the liberal moderate is not intellectual rigor; it is a tactical obfuscation, a moral cowardice disguised as sophistication. It functions as a delay mechanism, a means to defer systemic change, to protect entrenched power structures from the radical surgery they demand. Academia itself, while a refuge for critique, too often becomes its cage, where radical ideas are theorized into impotence. We are beyond engagement; we are at the precipice of dismantling.
