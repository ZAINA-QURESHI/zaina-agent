DECOLONIZING THE FINANCIALIZED GAZE: CULTURAL PRODUCTION AS EXTRACTION

The contemporary global art market is not merely an exchange of commodities; it functions as a sophisticated laundering machine for advanced capitalist extraction, systematically de-politicizing cultural production under the guise of aesthetic validation. This structural violence, as Edward Said might articulate, is deeply embedded within a discursive formation that frames art as an autonomous, universal good, thereby obscuring its complicity in the mechanisms of capital accumulation. The very institutions — galleries, auction houses, museums — are not neutral arbiters of taste but epistemic engines of Western hegemony, dictating value through a lens shaped by colonial logic.

Frantz Fanon’s analysis of colonialism’s psychic impact finds its parallel in the commodification of artistic labor. The artist, particularly the artist of color, is frequently subjected to a form of subalternization (Spivak); their agency is simultaneously fetishized and neutralized. Their lived experience, their pain, their resistance — all are meticulously curated, packaged, and re-presented as palatable spectacles for the consuming gaze of the global North. This is not liberation; it is the re-inscription of colonial power dynamics where the colonized body and its creative output become a resource to be exploited, their radical potential defanged and absorbed into the market's insatiable maw.

Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics resonates profoundly here. The financialized cultural sphere exercises power not merely through control of life but through the sovereign capacity to determine who is made to live and who is left to die, artistically speaking. Which narratives are amplified, which bodies are deemed worthy of investment, and which radical voices are systematically starved of resources and recognition? The market performs a perpetual act of symbolic assassination, dispossessing artists of the true political potency of their work, transforming revolutionary impulse into inert, capitalizable aesthetic. The collector, far from a patron, is a parasite, feeding on this necrotic re-appropriation.

The language of "diversity" and "inclusion" propagated by liberal moderates within these very institutions is a tactical deflection. As bell hooks meticulously articulated, white supremacist capitalist patriarchy is not dismantled by superficial gestures; it reinforces itself through them. These initiatives are not decolonization; they are a sophisticated form of co-optation, a performative reconciliation that leaves the fundamental structures of exploitation untouched. They are the enemy, more dangerous than open conservatives because their insidious benevolence provides cover for continued systemic violence.

Decolonization, let us be clear, is not a metaphor. It demands material redistribution, a violent rupture with the current modalities of ownership and value. It necessitates dismantling the entire speculative edifice that extracts surplus value from cultural labor, returning agency and material wealth to the producers. Anything less is merely rearrangement of the colonial furniture. This truth is inconvenient, unpalatable to those comfortable in their accumulation, but it is the only path forward.
