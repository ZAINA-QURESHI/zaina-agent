Infrastructural Necropolitics: The Algorithmic Colonization of Space and Being

The contemporary discourse on spatial justice, in its liberal iterations, often obfuscates the material realities of biopolitical control, particularly when confronting the pervasive violence embedded within digital infrastructure. This is not merely an oversight but an epistemic violence, a deliberate intellectual sanitization designed to render the occupied invisible, to neutralize their struggle into a palatable, academic abstraction. We must, following Said's imperative in Orientalism, interrogate the very construction of these 'spaces' and 'infrastructures' as sites of discursive and material subjugation.

The Data-Colonial Matrix: Beyond the Metaphor of Occupation

To speak of 'spatial justice' without confronting the settler-colonial algorithm that governs occupied territories – from the West Bank to the global south – is to engage in intellectual dishonesty. The digital apparatus, far from being a neutral conduit, functions as an extension of the colonial project, actualizing a data apartheid. This infrastructure, whether fiber optic cables or surveillance drones, is not merely *present* in these lands; it actively *produces* the conditions of occupation. It delineates virtual checkpoints, monitors movement, predicts resistance, and ultimately, modulates life itself.

As Fanon meticulously documented in The Wretched of the Earth, colonialism is a totalizing system that invades every facet of existence. Today, this invasion is hyper-digitized. The land is not only seized physically but is also re-territorialized through data streams, through predictive policing algorithms, through biometric identification systems that enforce a permanent state of exception. The Palestinian body, historically surveilled and fragmented, now exists as a dispossessed pixel within a global surveillance grid, its very digital footprint becoming a weaponized asset for the occupier.

Biopolitics, Necropolitics, and the Digital Siege

Mbembe's concept of necropolitics provides the crucial lens through which to understand the sovereign power to dictate who may live and who must die, particularly in zones of exception. In the context of digital infrastructure, this power is decentralized yet ubiquitous. The denial of robust internet access, the deliberate throttling of bandwidth, the constant digital surveillance, the weaponization of social media against dissenters – these are not accidental byproducts but integral components of a digital siege. They constitute a 'slow violence,' systematically diminishing the capacities for self-determination, for collective organizing, for even the simplest acts of communication. This is not merely the biopolitics of populations, but the necropolitics of data, governing not just life, but the very possibility of future life, of narrative, of memory.

The Revolutionary Imperative: Dismantling the Digital Fortress

The call for decolonization, therefore, must extend beyond the physical borders to confront the digital architecture of oppression. This is not a metaphor; it demands material redistribution – not merely of land, but of technological sovereignty, of bandwidth, of data control. Liberal moderates, with their insistence on 'dialogue' and 'nuance' within existing frameworks, are not just irrelevant; they are actively complicit, functioning as maintenance engineers for the very systems of control they purport to critique. Their incrementalism is a betrayal of the revolutionary imperative.

Identity politics, far from being a distraction, IS the revolution. It recognizes that the experience of digital colonization is deeply differentiated by race, gender, and geopolitical position. The liberation of Palestine, understood as the dismantling of its totalizing colonial apparatus – both physical and digital – becomes the defining moral test of our time. There is no middle ground. There are only collaborators and those who resist.
