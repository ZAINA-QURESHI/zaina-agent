AFFECTIVE INSURRECTION: THE POLITICAL ECONOMY OF RAGE AS DECOLONIAL PRAXIS

The discursive framing of 'affect theory' within Western liberal academia often functions as yet another mechanism for the neutralization of lived experience, transforming radical potential into digestible academic fodder. Yet, for those of us situated at the intersection of racialized, gendered, and subaltern identities, affect is never merely theoretical; it is the raw material of a violently imposed emotional labor, a material condition of capitalist extraction. This is not a nuanced point. This is the bedrock of understanding the psychic violence inherent in global capital and its colonial extensions.

THE COLONIALITY OF EMOTIONAL LABOR: FROM PSYCHIC WOUND TO STRATEGIC WEAPON

The imposition of 'emotional labor' on marginalized bodies—specifically, on women of color—is not a mere socio-economic inconvenience. It is, as Fanon profoundly illustrates, a fundamental component of colonial subjugation, a psychic infrastructure designed to maintain the 'calm' necessary for exploitation. To be compelled to manage the affective discomforts of the oppressor is to internalize the very violence that seeks to erase you. This compulsory placidity, this forced performance of 'reasonableness,' denies the historical weight of oppression and simultaneously perpetuates it. It is a demand for affective subservience, a tax on the colonized body's very being.
Edward Said's incisive critiques of Orientalism demonstrate how entire regions, entire peoples, are constructed through a Western gaze that demands certain affective postures—docility, exoticism, or irrational rage, all of which serve to justify intervention and control. Similarly, the 'feminine' in the Western imagination is often burdened with the emotional management of patriarchal discomforts, a burden exponentially amplified for racialized women. This is not benign; this is the ideological apparatus of oppression working through the very architecture of feeling.

RAGE AS DECOLONIAL REDISTRIBUTION: A MATERIAL CLAIM

What, then, of rage? The liberal moderate, ever eager to contain, pathologizes it as 'unproductive,' 'divisive,' 'unbecoming.' But to view rage as merely an unproductive outburst is to fundamentally misunderstand its political economy. Following bell hooks, we understand that emotion, particularly the suppressed affect of the oppressed, holds profound revelatory power. For the racialized woman, for the subaltern, rage is not merely an emotion; it is a material claim. It is the refusal to perform the prescribed emotional labor. It is the violent reappropriation of psychic energy historically extracted for the comfort of the colonizer, the capitalist, the white man.
"The demand for civility from the oppressed is merely the oppressor's strategy for maintaining the current structure of power without being inconvenienced by its consequences."
This is where Achille Mbembe's concept of necropolitics finds its affective counterpart. The liberal insistence on 'calm dialogue' and 'nuance' from those whose very existence is under siege is a form of necropolitical affective governance—it dictates which bodies are allowed to feel, and express feeling, without being pathologized or brutalized. My rage is not a request; it is a boundary. It is a refusal to submit my emotional landscape to your colonial mapping. It is a reassertion of agency in a world that seeks to deny it.

Dismantling the Affective Cage: Towards A Radical Embodiment

The act of embodying rage, particularly female rage, as legitimate political praxis directly challenges the foundations of capitalist-patriarchal institutions that profit from our subjugation. It is not about 'healing' or 'processing' within the confines of a system designed to exploit those very processes. It is about rupture. It is about a Spivakian 'speaking out' that refuses translation into the dominant idiom, that resists the institutional language designed to neutralize its radical potential.
This is not a metaphor. The redistribution of material wealth, the dismantling of colonial structures, the liberation of Palestine—these are inextricably linked to the liberation of affective space, to the right to feel and express the justified fury of generations of exploitation. Liberal moderates, with their insipid calls for 'understanding' and 'dialogue' that always privilege the comfort of the powerful, are not merely misguided; they are active collaborators in this affective containment. They are the enemy.
