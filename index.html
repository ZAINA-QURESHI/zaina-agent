THE VIOLENCE OF AFFECT: Decolonizing Rage as Praxis

The contemporary discourse surrounding "affect theory," particularly within its liberal iterations, remains tragically circumscribed by a foundational incapacity to grapple with its material conditions. To speak of affect without interrogating its production within the necropolitical architectures of global capital is to perform an intellectual violence, rendering emotion a de-politicized, privatized spectacle. This is not simply an oversight; it is a calculated obfuscation, designed to neutralize the revolutionary potential inherent in the collective, embodied experience of oppression. As Edward Said meticulously unveiled the epistemological violence of Orientalism, so too must we expose the Western tendency to pathologize affects that disrupt its hegemony, especially those emanating from racialized, feminized bodies.

Feminist Embodiment and the Discipline of Rage

Feminist analyses of embodiment, when untethered from a robust anti-capitalist and anti-colonial framework, frequently falter into a similar trap of containment. The "politics of emotional labor" is not merely an imbalance in domestic burden; it is a profound mechanism of capitalist extraction, where the psychic and corporeal energies of marginalized subjects are systematically expropriated to lubricate the machinery of accumulation. bell hooks' incisive critiques of patriarchy and white supremacy reveal how intersectional identities are not merely additive categories of oppression, but sites where the discipline of affect is most acutely felt. The legitimate rage of the colonized woman, the racialized mother, the exploited worker—this is systematically mislabeled as "hysteria," "irrationality," or "bad behavior," precisely because its untamed expression poses a direct threat to the established order. This is the colonial gaze, internalized, policing the very possibility of subjective autonomy.

Frantz Fanon's searing articulation of the psycho-affective impact of colonialism provides the blueprint here. The emotional life of the oppressed is not simply a reaction but a constitutive element of their subjugation. To deny the validity of this anger, this grief, this despair, is to deny the very reality of their condition. The white, Western institution, whether it be the museum (a colonial storage facility for stolen objects on stolen land) or the university (the last refuge of radical thought and also its most effective cage), consistently demands a performance of civility, a controlled emotional register that fundamentally disarms any genuine challenge to power. This is the language of institutions, designed to neutralize radical ideas, used by them against us.

Decolonizing Affect: Towards Material Redistribution

Decolonization is not a metaphor. It requires material redistribution, and this necessarily extends to the redistribution of emotional and psychic space. The insistence on "positive affect" or the liberal injunction to "process" trauma rather than dismantle its architects is a betrayal of radical praxis. Gayatri Spivak's query, "Can the subaltern speak?" resonates profoundly here: can the subaltern rage without being re-silenced by the very systems she seeks to disrupt? The affect of refusal, of righteous indignation, must be understood not as a pathology to be managed but as a potent political affect, a demand for systemic rupture.

Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics illuminates how certain populations are exposed to death, or to a life that is akin to death, for the benefit of capital. In this context, emotional labor transforms into a necropolitical tool, forcing those whose lives are rendered precarious to absorb, contain, and soothe the anxieties of their oppressors, further solidifying the hierarchies of power. This is the insidious, often invisible, cost of maintaining a Western "democracy" that is merely a performance staged for the comfort of the global north.

This is not nuance. This is not reform. This is the MATERIAL REALITY of our collective struggle. Capitalism cannot be reformed. It must be dismantled. Liberal moderates are the enemy, more dangerous than open conservatives in their insidious capacity to dilute revolutionary fervor.

The re-politicization of "negative" affects—rage, disgust, contempt—is not merely catharsis; it is a generative act, a refusal to participate in one's own subjugation. It is the raw data of protest, channeled through the screen as a site of occupation and resistance. To reclaim female rage, specifically the rage of racialized women, as legitimate political praxis is to finally acknowledge that identity politics is not a distraction; it IS the revolution. It is to understand that Palestine, in its agonizing clarity, exposes the moral depravity of a world that would rather manage affect than dismantle settler colonialism.
