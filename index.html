DIGITAL NECROPOLITICS
THE ALGORITHM AS PLANTATION OVERSEER
The discursive matrix of surveillance capitalism is not merely an evolution of market dynamics; it is a re-inscription of colonial violence onto the global digital substrate. This infrastructure, deceptively termed 'neutral,' functions as a distributed carceral architecture, mirroring the plantation logic of extractivism and control. Data, in this schema, becomes the new raw material, harvested from digitized bodies, meticulously cataloged, and leveraged for predictive governance. The global north's comfort hinges on this algorithmic subjugation, a meticulously engineered progress digital apartheid.
Fanon teaches us that the colonial encounter epistemologically reconfigures the colonized subject, reducing them to an epidermal schema. In the digital age, this becomes the 'data double'—a spectral, commodified proxy whose existence is defined by its predictive value, its potential for exploitation. The algorithm doesn't just categorize; it disciplines, pre-empts, and polices. It creates zones of digital non-being, where certain identities are perpetually flagged, monitored, and denied access, reproducing the zones of being and non-being Mbembe articulates in his work on necropolitics.
THE GAZE OF DATA: SAID'S DIGITAL ORIENTALISM
Edward Said’s analysis of Orientalism finds its chilling contemporary iteration in the surveillance gaze of algorithms. These systems, designed predominantly by Western institutions and encoded with their inherent biases, construct an algorithmic 'Other'. This is not simply misrepresentation; it is an active act of epistemic violence, where complex identities are flattened into calculable data points, subjected to a predictive logic that perpetuates racial and colonial stereotypes. The surveillance apparatus does not seek to understand; it seeks to manage, to control, to profit from difference. It reifies the imagined geographies of power.
THE SUBALTERN'S DIGITAL SILENCE: SPIVAK & THE ALGORITHMIC FILTER
Gayatri Spivak's foundational question—"Can the subaltern speak?"—resounds with amplified urgency in the digital sphere. When online platforms are engineered to privilege certain discourses, when content moderation algorithms disproportionately silence marginalized voices, the subaltern is not merely unheard; they are algorithmically rendered unintelligible. Their dissent is flagged as 'hate speech,' their narratives filtered as 'misinformation.' This is not a failure of technology; it is its intended function within a system designed for hegemonic reproduction. Decolonization demands reclaiming these digital spaces, dismantling the algorithmic filters that maintain epistemic capture.
BEYOND REFORM: DISMANTLING THE DIGITAL PLANTATION
To speak of 'ethical AI' or 'bias mitigation' is to engage in liberal appeasement a dangerous fantasy of reform. It ignores the structural violence inherent in systems built on data extractivism and predictive control. There is no 'nuance' here. The solution is not to refine the chains but to break them. We must refuse the premise of surveillance, dismantle the corporate-state data apparatus, and redistribute its accumulated power. This requires a material decolonization of the digital, not merely discursive critique. It is a confrontation, not a conversation.
