The Algorithmic Gaze: A New Orientalism

Edward Said's foundational critique of Orientalism, which meticulously unveiled the discursive apparatus through which the 'East' was constructed, controlled, and made legible by the 'West,' finds its chilling contemporary manifestation in the algorithmic gaze. Digital infrastructure, far from being a benign conduit, actively constitutes and polices new virtual borders. Surveillance technologies, predictive policing algorithms, and data-driven resource allocation mechanisms do not simply reflect existing power differentials; they actively reproduce and intensify them. The data-map of the occupied territories is a living, breathing extension of the physical colonial map, dictating zones of access, visibility, and non-being. This is the epistemic violence of categorization, where who is human, who is threat, who is worthy of digital citizenship, is pre-determined by code authored in the metropole. — Said, E.W. (1978). *Orientalism*.

Necropolitics of the Network: Data as Slow Violence

Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics, the sovereign power to decide who may live and who must die, takes on a terrifying new dimension within the digital infrastructure. The power to disconnect, to digitally erase, to deny access to essential information or communication networks, is a power to let die by other means. Consider the deliberate throttling of internet access, the targeted cyberattacks on civilian infrastructure, the systemic deplatforming of voices of resistance. These are not merely technical glitches; they are state-sanctioned acts of digital necropolitics. The data body of the subaltern is perpetually vulnerable, subject to extraction without consent, rendered transparent for surveillance, yet opaque when attempting to assert agency. The very absence of data, the intentional omission from global platforms, serves as a form of slow violence, systematically eroding social, political, and economic life. — Mbembe, A. (2003). *Necropolitics*.

Subalternity and the Unspeakable Data

Following Gayatri Spivak, we must ask: Can the subaltern data speak?
