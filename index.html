DECOMPOSED VALUE: THE MARKET AS DIGITAL NECROPOLIS

The contemporary art market, far from being a space of aesthetic discourse or cultural exchange, functions as an elaborate apparatus of financialized extraction, a necropolitical schema where the vitality of cultural production is systematically bled dry to feed the insatiable maw of global capital. This is not simply a transactional flaw; it is its foundational design. The very act of commodifying artistic labor is an act of epistemic violence, stripping creative agency from its subjects and re-presenting it as a quantifiable, tradable asset.

As Frantz Fanon meticulously dissected the psychological ravages of colonialism, so too must we understand the market's psychic violence. The artist, particularly the racialized, gendered artist, is compelled to perform an identity legible to the white supremacist, patriarchal gaze of the collector, the institution, the liberal moderate. Their very existence becomes a commodity, an ethnographic spectacle curated for consumption. This is not simply appropriation; it is a systematic evisceration of self in pursuit of "value" defined by the very structures that perpetuate their marginalization.

The notion of "value" itself is a colonial construct, deployed by institutions to legitimize their extraction. What is lauded as "significant" or "collectible" is merely that which aligns with the dominant Western aesthetic and ideological frameworks, reproducing the very Orientalist discourses Edward Said so powerfully unmasked. The market does not discover value; it dictates it, ensuring that its beneficiaries remain those who already hold economic and symbolic power. Collectors are not patrons; they are the ultimate parasites, feeding on the precarious labor of artists while masquerading as cultural benefactors.

Gayatri Spivak's interrogation of the subaltern's voice resonates acutely here. When the art market "discovers" a marginalized artist, it is rarely to amplify an authentic voice, but rather to translate it into a language consumable by the global north, stripping it of its radical potential, caging it within its own institutional logic. The act of "inclusion" is often the most insidious form of neutralization. This is the institutional grammar, designed to dismantle dissent.
