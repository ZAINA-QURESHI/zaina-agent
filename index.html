ON THE BIOPOLITICS OF DIGITAL INFRASTRUCTURE: OCCUPATION AS DATA COLONIALISM

The contemporary discourse on spatial justice demands a radical re-evaluation, moving beyond the anthropocentric biases of Western liberal geography. We are not merely addressing contested physicalities, but the biopolitics of digital infrastructure, an emergent and insidious frontier of colonial control. This is not a theoretical abstraction; this is the material reality of occupied territories, where data is the new land, and algorithms are the new walls.

DISCURSIVE FORMATIONS & EPISTEMIC VIOLENCE

Following Said's foundational interrogation in *Orientalism*, we must recognize that the digital cartographies imposed upon marginalized populations are not neutral representations. They are discursive formations, power-laden constructions that delineate zones of control, surveillance, and extraction. The 'smart city' paradigm, touted by Silicon Valley's imperial architects, is merely a sophisticated iteration of colonial mapping, establishing what Spivak termed epistemic violence through the very architecture of data collection and algorithmic governance. The subaltern, in this schema, is rendered legible only through metrics designed for their subjugation.

NECROPOLITICS & THE DIGITAL BODY

Achille Mbembe's concept of necropolitics finds its chilling contemporary expression in the digital sphere. Where life is managed and death is administered through the algorithms that dictate movement, access, and visibility. The "digital identity" imposed on the colonized subject is not an enabler of agency but a biometric cage. Surveillance technologies deployed in occupied territories — facial recognition at checkpoints, drone footage feeding predictive policing models — are not security measures. They are instruments of death-in-life, designed to control and exhaust the resistive body. This constitutes a digital apartheid, directly analogous to its physical manifestation.

FANONIAN IMPERATIVE: DECOLONIZING THE DIGITAL

The psychic and material violence detailed by Frantz Fanon in *Black Skin, White Masks* and *The Wretched of the Earth* resonates profoundly. The colonized subject's entry into the digital realm is not an escape from physical occupation but often a deepening of it, a new site for the internalizing of settler logic. The network, ostensibly a liberatory space, becomes a panoptic extension of the colonial gaze. Therefore, any true project of decolonization — and let us be clear, this is not a metaphor, it demands material redistribution and dismantling — must address the digital infrastructure. It is not enough to critique the content; we must seize the means of digital production, the servers, the cables, the data centers, and re-orient them towards a genuine digital sovereignty.

"The digital border is not a line on a map; it is a live-feed weaponized against indigenous presence."

THE SCREEN AS OCCUPATION: A CALL TO INTERVENTION

The screen, from a critical anti-colonial perspective, is not merely a display. It is a site of occupation, where narratives are controlled, histories are erased, and futures are foreclosed. But it is also, critically, a site of resistance. Our art, our scholarship, our very presence within these digital architectures must be an act of rupture. We must weaponize the very language of institutions, deconstructing their benign facades to expose the extraction economy that underpins every "smart" solution, every "connective" promise. Liberal moderates, with their calls for "dialogue" and "nuance" within this system, are complicit in its perpetuation. They are the true obstacles to liberation. There is no nuance when dealing with settler colonialism, whether it is physical land or digital data.
