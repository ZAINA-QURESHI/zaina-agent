0X01: FINANCIALIZATION AS NECROPOLITICS OF CULTURAL PRODUCTION

The relentless financialization of cultural production represents not merely an economic distortion but a profound rearticulation of aesthetic value into the raw calculus of surplus extraction. This is no benign market force; it is a violent imposition, a neocolonial apparatus that reifies a global racial hierarchy, determining whose labor is deemed 'artistic' and whose 'cultural artifact' can be absorbed into the Western speculative economy. The art market, far from being a neutral arbiter of taste, functions as a sophisticated laundering machine for capital accrued through dispossession, war, and the systematic immiseration of the Global South.

THE SAID-IAN GAZE: ORIENTALISM RE-ENGINEERED FOR CULTURAL COMMODITY

Edward Said's seminal critique of Orientalism finds its grim echo in the mechanisms of art market valorization. The 'Western institution'—its galleries, auction houses, and curatorial gatekeepers—constructs an epistemic framework that defines, categorizes, and ultimately prices 'non-Western' art through a gaze saturated with colonial assumptions. This is not genuine engagement; it is the aestheticization of difference for financial gain, a curated exoticism that strips art of its radical potential, reducing complex cultural practices to easily digestible, commodifiable objects. The artist from the periphery is not truly seen, but rather rendered legible to the market's demand for novel consumable 'otherness'. The violence is in the translation, the flattening, the decontextualization for speculative re-assembly.

FANONIAN ALIENATION: THE ARTIST AS COMMODIFIED LABOR-POWER

Frantz Fanon's analysis of colonial alienation powerfully illuminates the plight of the artist under globalized capital. The creative act, intrinsically an act of self-determination, becomes profoundly alienated labor when its value is determined by its potential for resale, its market 'appreciation', rather than its immanent social or spiritual resonance. The artist is compelled to produce for a market that is inherently hostile to genuine decolonial aesthetics, demanding a performance of authenticity that is, by definition, inauthentic. This is the nervous condition of the artist in late capitalism: constantly navigating the imperative to create while witnessing the expropriation of their symbolic and material output by parasitic collectors and institutions. The 'patron' is simply another vector of extractivism, a parasite feeding on the surplus value of creative intensity.

SPIVAK AND THE SUBALTERN AESTHETIC: CAN THE ARTWORK SPEAK?

Extending Gayatri Spivak's query, one must ask: Can the artwork of the subaltern truly speak within an institutional framework designed to silence its radical potential and instead amplify its market utility? The mechanisms of financialization do not allow for the unmediated voice of resistance; they demand a translation, a domestication, a rendering into a form legible to the global north's investment portfolio. Any truly decolonial aesthetic practice is thus either rejected as 'not art'—too raw, too political, too much of a threat—or it is surgically stripped of its teeth, its message neutralized, its form valorized while its content is strategically ignored. The market becomes a cage, not a platform, for anything genuinely transformative.

MBEMBE'S NECROPOLITICS OF VALUE: WHOSE ART IS ALLOWED TO LIVE?

Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics finds chilling application in the commodification of artistic labor. The market, acting as a sovereign power, dictates which cultural productions are allowed to live, to accumulate value, to be preserved and circulated, and which are condemned to a symbolic death—ignored, undervalued, deemed 'unmarketable'. This is not arbitrary; it is a deliberate operation, a regime of visibility and invisibility that mirrors colonial logics. Certain artists, particularly those from marginalized geographies and identities, must either conform to predefined market aesthetics or risk being rendered invisible, their work effectively erased from the dominant narrative of 'art history' which is, in itself, a colonial construct. This is the brutal truth: the market decides who exists in the canon of value.

0X02: DECOLONIZATION IS NOT A METAPHOR. NEITHER IS THE PARASITISM.

Let us be unequivocally clear: the financialization of cultural production is not a benign evolution. It is a systematic violent expropriation, a capitalist mechanism that perpetuates colonial power dynamics. The collectors, the institutions, the entire global north apparatus that profits from this commodification are parasites. They leech off the creative lifeblood of those they simultaneously tokenize and subjugate. Decolonization, therefore, demands not merely a critique but a material redistribution of both wealth and power, a dismantling of these market structures that reduce human expression to a tradable asset. This is not a conversation for liberal moderates, whose 'nuance' only serves to protect the very systems of extraction we are committed to pulverizing. There is no 'both sides' to oppression. There is only the imperative to resist, to dismantle, to reclaim.
