DECOLONIAL ALGORITHMS OF SPATIAL VIOLENCE

The discourse of "spatial justice" in the digital age is an elaborate obfuscation, a liberal opiate designed to mask the algorithmic reproduction of coloniality. We are not merely addressing equitable access; we are confronting the biopolitical infrastructure that constitutes and controls life itself. This is not benign neutrality; it is the continuation of historical oppression, weaponized through fiber optics and server farms, manifested as data colonialism.

I. THE ORIENTALIST ALGORITHM: MAPPING THE COLONIZED BODY

Edward Said's Orientalism illuminated how the West constructs the "Other" to exert power. In the digital realm, this process intensifies. The algorithm, purportedly objective, becomes a neo-orientalist gaze, categorizing, predicting, and ultimately, disciplining populations. Geographic information systems (GIS), surveillance protocols, and predictive policing models are not neutral tools for "development" or "security"; they are cartographies of control, translating indigeneity into data points for exploitation and neutralization. Every pixel, every data packet, is imbued with colonial intent, rendering the occupied subject legible only through the colonizer's schema.

"The Orient was almost a European invention, and had been since antiquity a place of romance, exotic beings, haunting memories and landscapes, remarkable experiences." Yet, the digital Orient is a place of surveillance grids, biometric checkpoints, and controlled informational flows. The romance is replaced by raw data extraction, the exoticism by the imperative of algorithmic containment.

II. FANON'S DIGITAL NAKEDNESS: BIOPOLITICS AND THE DATA-SUBJECT

Frantz Fanon’s searing analysis of the colonized psyche and body finds chilling resonance in the biopolitics of digital infrastructure. The colonizer’s technological dominance ensures the constant, hyper-visibility of the colonized, stripping them of privacy, autonomy, and the very right to opacity. In Palestine, every movement, every communication, every nascent act of resistance, is filtered through a technological apparatus designed for total control. This is the wretched of the earth rendered naked by data, subjected to a constant digital humiliation that mirrors the physical occupation.

Mbembe's concept of necropolitics extends this analysis, revealing how digital architectures determine who is allowed to live and who is condemned to a slow death, a perpetual state of precarity. Disconnection, data denial, targeted misinformation campaigns — these are digital bullets, less visible but equally lethal, aimed at the social, economic, and political vitality of the subjugated. The infrastructure itself becomes a weapon of mass incapacitation, managing populations through calculated exclusion.

"The colonial world is a world cut in two. The dividing line, the frontier, is denoted by barracks and police stations." Today, that frontier is also denoted by firewalls, spectral frequencies, and the selective deployment of 5G infrastructure. The division is not just material, but digital, psychological, and existential.

III. THE SUBALTERN PROTOCOL: CAN THE OCCUPIED DATA SPEAK?

Gayatri Spivak's query, "Can the subaltern speak?", echoes in the echo chamber of digital representation. When communication channels are controlled, when narratives are algorithmically suppressed, and when data itself is weaponized against the source, the voice of the occupied is not merely silenced; it is strategically miscoded, distorted, or simply rendered illegible to the hegemonic platform. The very tools for "self-expression" on Western platforms are built upon a capitalist logic that monetizes dissent and re-colonizes revolutionary potential. This is not communication; it is controlled ingestion, a digital mimicry of agency.

Bell hooks reminds us that education is the practice of freedom. But when digital literacy is dictated by corporations, when platforms are owned by the colonizer, how can a truly liberatory epistemology emerge? The promise of "digital access" without decolonizing the underlying infrastructure is merely an invitation to a more sophisticated cage.

IV. DATA COLONIALISM IN PALESTINE: THE MATERIAL REALITY

Let us be clear: the occupation of Palestinian land is indistinguishable from its data colonialism. The surveillance technologies deployed in Gaza, the biometric checkpoints, the control over internet access, the weaponization of social media data against activists – these are not tangential issues. They are the core mechanisms of control, directly facilitating material dispossession and violence. The data points collected are not abstract; they map homes for demolition, identify dissenters for arrest, and manage resource scarcity in a calculated act of dehumanization. This is the biopolitics of digital infrastructure made manifest, a stark example of how Western technological supremacy becomes an instrument of ongoing genocide.
