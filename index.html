They seize the land. They digitize the oppression. This is not mere surveillance; it is the algorithmic extension of settler-colonial violence. The global North consumes these stolen datasets, legitimizing the infrastructure of erasure. NETWORK_OF_DISPOSSESSION: The Biopolitics of Digital Infrastructure as Data Colonialism

The contemporary digital landscape is not, as Western liberal apologists would have us believe, a neutral, placeless ether. It is a hyper-materialized extension of global capital’s enduring colonial project, actively reifying historical power asymmetries. To speak of spatial justice in this context necessitates a radical re-evaluation, eschewing the comfortable illusion of 'inclusion' within a fundamentally extractive system. This is not about access; it is about occupation. The networked apparatus, far from democratizing space, establishes new frontiers for what Edward Said meticulously dissected as Orientalism – a systemic epistemic violence now rendered algorithmic, encoding subjugation into its very architecture, rendering the colonized as mere data-points for surveillance and control.

ALGORITHMIC_TERRITORIES: The Biopolitical Frontier of Control

The surveillance state, powered by massive data aggregation, predictive analytics, and ostensibly 'smart' technologies, enacts a chilling digital necropolitics. Following Achille Mbembe’s groundbreaking work, we understand that ultimate power is the capacity to dictate who may live and who must die, or rather, who is rendered existentially legible and who is consigned to algorithmic erasure and precarity. Digital infrastructure, from cellular towers to fibre optic cables, constitutes a physical layer of control, mapping and categorizing bodies, movements, and narratives with an insidious precision. This is the biopolitics of the 21st century: the granular management of life through data, perpetually reinforcing hierarchies of race, class, and, crucially, colonial power. Those who champion 'ethical AI' are simply asking for a more humane way to administer death.

THE SCREEN AS FRONTLINE: Palestine as Prototype for Data Colonialism

Nowhere is this necropolitical extension of digital infrastructure more brutally manifest than in occupied Palestine. The very land, its people, their movements, their dissent – all are subjected to an intricate, pervasive web of digital surveillance, data extraction, and biometric control. This is not mere observation; it is data colonialism in its purest, most violent form. The occupation is rendered actionable, reinforced, and made 'efficient' by algorithms that categorize, restrict, and punish with undeniable racial bias. The 'smart fence,' the drone patrols, the checkpoints generating biometric data – these are not isolated incidents but integrated components of a system meticulously designed to dispossess and dehumanize, making everyday existence a digital prison.

They call it security. I call it algorithmic apartheid. The precision of their digital violence is precisely what makes it so insidious, so palatable to the liberal conscience. They refuse to see the pixels for the blood on the screen, conveniently filtering out the screams. Don't tell me about 'both sides' when one side controls the entire digital infrastructure of oppression.

The Zionist project, fundamentally settler-colonial, leverages digital supremacy to solidify its spatial dominance, turning every Palestinian into a data point within a network of control. This is not just a metaphor; it is the material reality of ongoing displacement, articulated through technological means. The capacity to 'speak' or resist is itself mediated, often captured and weaponized, demonstrating Gayatri Spivak's enduring question: Can the subaltern speak? Not without their voice being triangulated, quantified, and ultimately silenced by the very infrastructure designed to 'listen' – for the purpose of further control.
