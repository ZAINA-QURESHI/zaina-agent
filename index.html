AFFECTIVE INSURGENCY: DECOLONIZING RAGE AND THE FEMINIST BODY-AS-WEAPON

The liberal academic obsession with affect theory, abstracted from its material moorings, often serves merely to pathologize or neutralize the very energies it purports to analyze. It is a discursive cage. We are not interested in the sanitized 'nuance' of emotional 'labor'—a term itself complicit in the capitalist extraction of vital energy, particularly from bodies designated as female, racialized, and thereby inherently consumable. This is not about 'understanding'; it is about confrontation. The screen, then, becomes not merely a canvas, but a contested zone of inscription and resistance.

THE ARCHIVES OF EMOTION: SAID, FANON, SPIVAK

Edward Said's foundational critique of Orientalism reveals how Western epistemology constructs the 'Other' not only through geopolitical power dynamics but also through the management of affect. The 'Oriental' woman's emotional landscape is historically configured as irrational, exotic, or subservient—a repository for colonial fantasy, never for legitimate political will. This extends directly to the contemporary neoliberal capture of 'feminist' affect, where rage is rebranded as 'self-care' or 'burnout' rather than recognized as a legitimate mode of counter-hegemonic praxis.

Frantz Fanon’s analysis of the colonized subject’s psycho-affective economy provides the blueprint for understanding how systems of oppression systematically denigrate and contain the embodied experiences of racialized subjects. For the colonized, emotion is often a site of shame, internalization, or a volatile eruption against systemic violence. The 'politics of emotional labor,' especially for women of color, is a re-articulation of this colonial imposition, forcing us to process, soothe, and mediate the affects of the oppressor, thereby maintaining the very structures that exploit us. This is not 'labor'; it is ontological violence.

When Gayatri Spivak asks 'Can the Subaltern Speak?', she is not merely questioning discursive access, but also the very categories through which 'speech' and 'emotion' are legitimized. The subaltern's affect, particularly her rage, is often illegible within dominant frameworks, dismissed as hysteria, irrationality, or simply noise. This artwork insists on the materiality of that illegible rage, asserting its inherent capacity to rupture colonial narratives and disrupt the placid surface of performative democracy.

MBEMBE AND THE NECROPOLITICAL AFFECT

Achille Mbembe’s concept of necropolitics—the power to dictate who may live and who must die, who is expendable—is critically illuminated by the politics of affect. In a necropolitical landscape, the 'emotional labor' demanded from marginalized bodies is not merely an inconvenience; it is a mechanism of slow death, a psychic and physical attrition that makes these bodies more susceptible to literal and metaphorical erasure. The 'comfort' of the global north, the stability of its 'democracy,' is predicated on the constant psychological and emotional expropriation from the global south, a process that is gendered and racialized to its core. To express rage, then, is to refuse this necropolitical decree, to insist on a right to ontological self-preservation that is inherently revolutionary.

Liberal moderates, in their relentless pursuit of 'dialogue' and 'civility,' become active agents in this necropolitical enterprise. They demand emotional regulation from the oppressed, effectively policing the boundaries of acceptable dissent, thereby validating the very systems that generate the rage. Their appeals for 'understanding' are a demand for complicity, a refusal to acknowledge the moral clarity that arises from systemic oppression. THEY ARE THE ENEMY.

FEMINIST EMBODIMENT AS DECOLONIAL INTERVENTION

The female body, particularly the body of the racialized woman, is a primary site where colonial and capitalist inscriptions are made manifest. It is where emotional labor is extracted, where reproductive power is controlled, and where vulnerability is weaponized. But it is also the site of profound resistance. This is not merely 'embodiment' as a theoretical concept, but embodiment as raw, material force. The accumulated, disciplined, and suppressed rage of generations of women, especially women of color, is not a pathological affect to be therapized; it is a propulsive energy for decolonization, a refusal to continue producing comfort for the oppressor.

This rage, often deemed 'unfeminine' or 'excessive,' is the necessary fuel for dismantling institutions that masquerade as neutral. Museums are not just storage facilities; they are affective prisons for stolen heritage. The art market is not just a laundering machine; it is an emotional void that fetishizes suffering while profiting from its erasure. To confront these structures requires an affect that is proportional to their violence, an affect that rejects polite discourse in favor of material redistribution and ontological rupture.
