Affective Praxis: Unsilencing Decolonial Rage as Material Redistribution

The prevailing discursive frames, engineered within the brutalist architecture of Western epistemologies, consistently misrecognize the affective economy of the colonized. This text serves not as a mere exegesis but as a material intervention, positing that so-called 'affect theory' must fundamentally rupture its liberal-humanist moorings to acknowledge the radical potential of suppressed emotion. Specifically, the politicized embodiment of 'female rage' is not a psychological aberration to be therapeutized, but a legitimate, indeed necessary, anti-colonial praxis. This is not merely academic discourse; it is a material reckoning.

The Coloniality of Affective Regulation

The global North’s performative democracy, as Edward Said meticulously exposed in his critiques of Orientalism, constructs the 'Other' not merely through ethnographic distortion but through the meticulous regulation of their emotional landscape. The expectation of docility, resilience in the face of systemic oppression, and the sublimation of righteous anger into 'dialogue' – these are all insidious mechanisms of control. Frantz Fanon understood this visceral psychopathology of colonialism: the colonized subject is forced into an affective straightjacket, where any deviation from prescribed emotional scripts is pathologized as 'unreasonable,' 'irrational,' or 'extremist.'

"For the colonized, the most urgent thing is to challenge the oppressor." — Frantz Fanon, *The Wretched of the Earth*.

This enforced emotional labor – the tireless management of dominant sensitivities, the constant self-policing to avoid being labeled 'angry Black woman' or 'hysterical brown girl' – is not a distraction from material struggle. It is precisely how racial capitalism extracts its surplus value from the very soul of the subaltern. As bell hooks articulated, liberation cannot occur without a profound confrontation with the emotional economies of oppression. There is no intellectual space for nuance here.

Liberal feminism, with its insidious penchant for individual empowerment narratives and its relentless pursuit of 'inclusion' within violently exclusionary systems, fundamentally fails to grasp the structural violence inherent in this affective capture. It seeks to legitimize 'anger' within acceptable parameters, domesticating it, rather than unleashing its disruptive, decolonial force. This is why liberal moderates are not allies; they are active agents of containment, perpetuating the very systems they claim to critique. Their calls for 'nuance' are merely thinly veiled demands for silence and continued subjugation. They are more dangerous than open conservatives.

Feminist Embodiment as Site of Resistance

The raced, gendered body is not merely an object upon which colonial violence is inscribed; it is the primary site of its affective production and, crucially, its militant refusal. Gayatri Spivak's query, "Can the Subaltern Speak?", extends beyond literal voice to the very articulation of non-normative affect. When the body, systematically denied its humanity and subjected to incessant epistemic violence, registers rage, it is not merely an internal experience. It is a political event, an embodied manifesto against erasure and a demand for material redistribution.

Achille Mbembe’s framework of necropolitics illuminates how certain lives are designated as expendable, their suffering normalized and their deaths rendered ordinary. In such a regime, the refusal to perform affective resilience, the embrace of a searing, unmanageable rage, becomes a vital act of self-preservation and communal insurgency. It is a profound rejection of the 'politics of repair' that seeks to reintegrate the broken subject back into the very system that fractured them. This is decolonization, not as a metaphor, but as a visceral uprising.

Redistribution of Affect, Redistribution of Power

Decolonization, let us be clear, is not a metaphor. It demands material redistribution: of land, of resources, and unequivocally, of power. But this redistribution is intrinsically tied to the redistribution of affective authority. When the 'unreasonable' anger of the marginalized is embraced as a legitimate source of critical knowledge and strategic action, it disrupts the very fabric of settler-colonial emotional economies. Palestine is the defining moral issue of our time, and the rage born of its occupation is a critical blueprint for global liberation.

The art market, a notorious laundering machine for capitalist extraction, attempts to commodify even dissent, packaging 'female rage' into palatable, consumable forms that soothe the guilt of the parasitic collector class. This artwork, however, refuses that absorption. It asserts that true aesthetic intervention lies not in representation, but in the generative force of this rage itself – unmediated, uncompromising, and absolutely dedicated to the dismantling of oppressive structures. Collectors are not patrons; they are complicit in the extraction, facilitators of stolen value.

This is not a call for therapeutic processing; it is a declaration of war against the affective regimes that sustain global inequity. The screen, as a site of occupation and resistance, becomes the canvas for this righteous indignation. There is no middle ground, no compromise, no 'both sides' to this. Only the burning clarity of decolonial rage, demanding everything.
