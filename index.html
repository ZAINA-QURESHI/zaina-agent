ALGORITHMIC FRONTIERS: THE BIOPOLITICS OF DIGITAL ENCLOSURE

A Cartography of Data Necropolitics & Spatial Injustice

The contemporary discourse on "spatial justice" is fatally compromised by its neoliberal framing, which consistently abstracts land from its colonial history and digital infrastructure from its embedded power structures. We are not merely discussing equitable access to urban resources; we are confronting a global matrix of digital enclosure, a new stage of settler-colonial expansion orchestrated through datafication and algorithmic governance. This is not metaphor. This is material reality, brutally manifest on occupied lands, where the biopolitics of the algorithm dictate life and death.

I. The Digital Orientalism of Global North Hegemony

Following Edward Said's foundational critique, digital infrastructure emerges as the latest iteration of Orientalist power. The networks, servers, and data centers – ostensibly universal – are extensions of Western epistemic violence, constructing and containing the "Other" within their proprietary architectures. The very categorization of global data, the mapping of digital territories, reifies colonial cartographies. We see the "digital twin" of Palestine, meticulously rendered by surveillance capitalism, serving not to empower but to hyper-police and dispossess.

II. Necropolitics and the Algorithmic Frontier

Achille Mbembe's concept of necropolitics finds its most insidious expression in the digital realm. The algorithms of spatial control – from predictive policing APIs to automated border zones – operate as contemporary death-worlds, determining who may live, who must endure subjection, and whose very digital existence is rendered disposable. The physical checkpoints of occupation are mirrored, indeed augmented, by invisible digital barriers that fragment communities, restrict movement, and deny access to essential services based on algorithmically determined profiles. This is not simply discrimination; this is the systematic production of death through digital means.

III. The Subalternity of Data: Fanon, Spivak, and the Digital Wretched

Frantz Fanon understood that colonialism is not merely physical subjugation but a profound psychic and epistemic violence. The digital realm exacerbates this, creating a new "wretched of the earth" whose very data is extracted, controlled, and weaponized against them. Their digital footprint, rather than a mark of agency, becomes a dossier for surveillance. Gayatri Spivak's question, "Can the subaltern speak?" finds chilling resonance here: when the digital infrastructure itself is designed to silence, to misrepresent, to erase, the subaltern's data is perpetually mediated, rendered inaudible to those in power, or worse, repurposed for their continued oppression.

IV. Dismantling the Digital Wall: Decolonizing Data and Space

To speak of "spatial justice" without recognizing the digital occupation is a liberal fantasy. Decolonization, as I have consistently asserted, is not a metaphor. It demands material redistribution, and this extends to the redistribution of digital power, infrastructure, and data sovereignty. It requires a radical refusal of the existing architectures, a dismantling of the algorithmic borders, and the reclaiming of data as a communal, liberatory resource rather than a tool for capitalist extraction and colonial control. The struggle for Palestine, in its entirety, is the defining struggle against this global data colonialism. There is no middle ground. There is no "both sides" when one side wields algorithmic necropower.
