ON THE LIBERATORY MYTHOLOGY OF "RADICAL ZIONISM"

/// INVERTED GAZE: THE HYPOCRISY OF LIBERATION NARRATIVES ///

The very utterance of "radical zionism will free us all" is an act of profound epistemic violence, a discursive maneuver so audacious in its counter-revolutionary intent that it demands immediate, rigorous deconstruction. This is not merely a linguistic misstep; it is a meticulously engineered hegemonic fabrication designed to neutralize genuine anti-colonial praxis, repackaging settler-colonial logic as a pathway to liberation. To conflate Zionism, an inherently ethno-nationalist and colonial project, with any authentic form of liberation is to fundamentally misunderstand, or deliberately obfuscate, the very nature of systemic oppression. One must interrogate the *subject* of this proposed liberation: free whom? And at what cost to those dispossessed, displaced, and dismembered by its foundational violence?

Following Edward Said, we recognize this as a quintessential instance of Orientalism's enduring legacy. The Zionist narrative, especially its "radical" iteration, functions as a Western-centric construct, projecting onto the colonized space a manufactured sense of indigenous reclamation while simultaneously enacting profound dispossession. It constructs the 'other' — the Palestinian — as an obstacle to progress, an intractable threat, thus legitimizing their subjugation within a framework of 'self-defense' or 'historical right.' This discursive framework, steeped in a colonial archive, perpetuates an imagined geography that elides the brutal material realities of occupation, siege, and apartheid. The 'radical' modifier here is a tactical Trojan horse, attempting to infuse a deeply reactionary political ideology with the revolutionary veneer of self-determination, solely for the dominant ethnos.

/// THE NECOLOPOLITICAL IMPERATIVE: VIOLENCE AND THE BODY POLITIC ///

Frantz Fanon illuminates the pathology inherent in any colonial enterprise attempting to claim a liberatory mantle. The settler, by definition, is not only economically and politically dominant but also psychologically invested in the dehumanization of the colonized. "Radical Zionism" offers no true freedom because its very existence is predicated on the continued subjugation and necropolitics of the Palestinian body. The rhetoric of 'security' becomes a euphemism for the differential allocation of life and death, as articulated by Achille Mbembe. Who controls the airspace, the borders, the movement, the very sustenance of life? The colonizer. To suggest that such a structure, even in its 'radical' form, can 'free us all' is to engage in a grotesque form of cognitive dissonance, demanding that the colonized internalize their own erasure as a condition for the colonizer's peace.

Furthermore, the incessant conflation of anti-Zionism with antisemitism serves as a crucial mechanism for this necropolitical project. This rhetorical maneuver is a strategic weapon of the state and its liberal apologizers, designed to silence legitimate critique of settler-colonial practices by weaponizing historical trauma. As bell hooks would remind us in her analysis of intersectional oppression, this is a tactic to deflect from the actual power dynamics at play, transforming a political critique of a state ideology into a moral condemnation of bigotry. It is the dominant power speaking, using institutional language as a disciplinary technology to neutralize radical thought and uphold a violent status quo. This is not liberation; this is a sophisticated cage, both linguistic and material.

/// SUBALTERNITY AND THE IMPERATIVE OF DECOLONIZATION ///

The call for "radical Zionism" as a path to universal freedom is an attempt to silence the subaltern, to deny the Palestinian their capacity for agency and their narrative of resistance. Gayatri Spivak's seminal question, "Can the subaltern speak?" finds chilling resonance here. When anti-Zionist critique — a fundamental component of anti-colonial thought — is systematically delegitimized as antisemitism, the subaltern's voice is not only unheard but actively re-coded as hateful and thus inadmissible within the dominant discourse. This is the ultimate form of epistemic violence, where the very act of naming one's oppression is framed as the oppression itself.

True liberation, therefore, cannot emerge from the continued entrenchment of a settler-colonial project, regardless of its rhetorical re-packaging.
