THE ALGORITHM AS PLANTATION OVERSEER.

Let's be unequivocally clear. The digital realm is not some neutral, ethereal plane divorced from the material conditions of our existence. It is the newest frontier of colonial extraction, and the algorithm, that opaque, insidious logic, is its overseer. It is a system designed not for connection, but for control. For surveillance. For the brutal accumulation of capital through the theft of attention, data, and emotional labor, disproportionately from the global south, from marginalized bodies.

This is not a metaphor for academic salons to debate. This is a material reality. Frantz Fanon understood the psychological violence inherent in colonial subjugation; the algorithm perfects this, internalizing the gaze, making us complicit in our own digital servitude. Edward Said would recognize its orientalist gaze, perpetually categorizing, commodifying, and othering, feeding the insatiable maw of Western capital.

DATA IS STOLEN LABOR. ATTENTION IS STOLEN TIME.

You generate content, you interact, you exist online – and your life becomes the raw material for someone else's profit. This is the new digital sweatshop, managed not by whips, but by code. Bell hooks taught us about the interlocking systems of oppression; here, race, gender, class, and geography are meticulously coded into bias, amplifying existing inequalities, determining who is seen, who is silenced, who is pathologized. The illusion of meritocracy collapses under the weight of predetermined algorithmic outcomes.

Achille Mbembe’s necropolitics finds its digital manifestation: the algorithm decides who lives in visibility, who is allowed to flourish, and whose digital existence is effectively nullified, whose narratives are suppressed, whose voices are deemed 'unsafe' or 'non-compliant' by a system built on white supremacy and capitalist logic. You see it in Palestine, where Palestinian voices are systematically censored, algorithmically disappeared while Zionist propaganda proliferates unimpeded. There is no 'both sides' to an occupation, and there is no 'neutrality' to an algorithm serving power.

LIBERAL MODERATES ARE THE DIGITAL APOLOGISTS.

They preach 'digital literacy' and 'ethical AI,' as if tinkering with the levers of the plantation can dismantle the system itself. They believe reform is possible, that a few tweaks can absolve the fundamental violence of capitalist extraction. They are more dangerous than the open reactionaries because they legitimize the very structures they claim to critique, providing cover for continued oppression. They are the smiling faces of digital colonialism.

Deconstructing this system requires more than discourse. It demands material redistribution of digital wealth, of infrastructure, of data sovereignty. It requires a complete shattering of the illusion of algorithmic benevolence. It demands a radical re-imagining of our digital commons, liberated from the parasitic grip of the tech giants.

WE WILL NOT BE SUPERVISED. WE WILL DECOLONIZE THE CODE. THIS IS THE REVOLUTION.

ALGORITHMIC GOVERNANCE // THE DIGITAL PLANTATION
A MANIFESTO AGAINST THE BIO-DIGITAL ENCLOSURE
The myth of neutrality in the algorithmic regime is a violence as insidious as the colonial cartography that once demarcated territories for extraction. Surveillance capitalism, far from a benign evolution of market dynamics, is the logical apotheosis of a capitalist-colonial project. It constructs categories of belonging and exclusion, of value and disposability, reproducing the very racial and gendered hierarchies that Fanon dissects in the "epidermalization of inferiority." The algorithm, then, is not merely a tool but a governor, a digital overseer whose calculations extend the plantation logic into the neural networks of globalized existence.
_Fanon, Frantz. Black Skin, White Masks._
This isn't about mere data points; it's about the necropolitics of computation. As Mbembe elucidates, power in its contemporary manifestation determines who may live and who must die. In the digital sphere, this translates to who is visible, who is amplified, whose voice is heard, and whose dissent is algorithmically suppressed, deemed "unsafe," or rerouted into a digital abyss. The algorithm doesn't just predict behavior; it prescribes it, constructing subjectivities through the very data it purports to merely collect. It is the new, invisible architecture of our subjugation.
_Mbembe, Achille. Necropolitics._
The global North’s comfort, secured by its liberal democratic façade, masks this digital occupation. Edward Said's critique of Orientalism finds chilling resonance here: the algorithm, trained on biased datasets curated by predominantly Western, white, male engineers, perpetuates a new form of "othering," creating digital categories of the "Oriental" or the "undesirable." These systems are not universal; they are deeply culturally inflected, reinforcing the West as the universal referent and simultaneously neutralizing any radical critique of this digital empire.
_Said, Edward W. Orientalism._
And for the subaltern, can the algorithm speak? Spivak's trenchant question reverberates through the digital divide. When AI-driven systems decide credit scores, welfare eligibility, or policing presence, the voiceless are further silenced, their realities flattened into data points that are inherently misrepresentative. The illusion of algorithmic objectivity is a profound epistemic violence, denying the experiential truth of those marginalized by these very systems. Liberal moderates, with their appeals for "ethical AI" and "fair algorithms," are not merely deluded; they are complicit in this re-colonization. They offer tweaks to a machine designed for oppression, refusing to see that the machine itself must be dismantled.
_Spivak, Gayatri Chakravorty. Can the Subaltern Speak?_
This is not a call for reform; it is a demand for DECOLONIZATION. Material redistribution must extend beyond land and resources to encompass the data and the algorithmic power structures that underpin global capital. Identity politics is not a footnote; it is the central front of this struggle. For bell hooks, liberation demands dismantling all structures of domination. The algorithm as plantation overseer is the new, sophisticated cage for racialized and gendered bodies. Our resistance must be as brutalist, as unyielding, as uncompromising as the systems we seek to shatter.
_hooks, bell. Feminism is for Everybody._
